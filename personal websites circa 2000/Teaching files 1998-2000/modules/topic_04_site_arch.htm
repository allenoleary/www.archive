Design Topic: Introduction to site architecture

Information design is nothing new. The Novel or enclyclopedia have very resolved and conventional information designs. (We'll have a look at those in class). Most of these conventions are to do with the handling of spatial layout.

The crucial difference between say book design and site design is that site design uses both space and time to acheive it's effects: Consider a building - not only to the architects have to think about the spatial relationships and functions of the volumes, but also how people will move through those volumes. Much effort goes into enabling people to get around the building in a logical and/or aesthetically pleasing way. Signage is a big deal in itself.

Think about the Melbourne Casino. The shopping strip that runs the north side of the ground floor is essentially modelled on a traditional city. Shops line each side of the progressional throughfare, just like a conventional street, while the food court tries to mirror the idea of a town square. Shops are organised around a peripheral edge, facing in to the central space. They've got it a bit wrong, becasue there is usually a central focus in a town square (a band rotunda, fountain or water pump), but in the Casino the central space is occupied by a lift and escalators.

New Media is beginning to have some conventions of it's own (top banner, navigation on the left-hand side, flat and deep structures).

There is also a growing cross-over between the web and other information rich environments like a newspaper. We'll take a look at that, and get the information order/visual order idea in our heads.

The Site Map

The site map is a graphical representation of the sites structure. It is not dissimilar to an architects floor plan. With a floor plan we look to see how much space each room has, as well as the possible pathways that we might travel through those spaces. Likewise with a site map it should clearly indicate the following things:

  • What the first page of your site is (entrance way)
  • What major sectional divisions exist in the information you are presenting, and how large each section is (volume)
  • Major pathways through your site (hallways and transitional spaces)
  • The position of each page on your site in the information architecture (plan)

Chunking

Chunking is the process of breaking information into appropriate sizes so it maintains its integrity and legibility. In print media the paragraph is the primary way of controlling informational chunks. In new media it tends to be the screen.
