2. Home Ownership in Australia

3. Government Policy

A popular line of argument in the 1970s and 1980s, which was given considerable prominence by Kemeny’s influential book, was that the desire for ownership is a response to government policy. He and others documented the many postwar policies which encouraged ownership and, by contrast, the relative absence of support for public and private rental. Regulated low mortgage interest rates, subsidies to first homebuyers and the absence of capital gains tax have all been identified as factors encouraging ownership. There is no doubt that such policies had their impact in reinforcing ownership in the postwar era, but they fail to explain why home ownership was important in earlier years when it had little government support.

The success of one tenure cannot be considered in isolation from others. If another tenure is perceived to be more attractive than home ownership for various reasons, then ownership may not prosper. This has not been the case in Australia. One reason for the lesser importance of ownership in many European societies has been the growth of social housing. With the size of this sector going up to around 40 per cent in, for example, the Netherlands and Sweden, home ownership by comparison just has to be squeezed out. In Australia, where political decisions have kept the social sector small, there was no threat to home ownership. This is of indirect importance to ownership, but still does not explain its dominance in the 20th century.
