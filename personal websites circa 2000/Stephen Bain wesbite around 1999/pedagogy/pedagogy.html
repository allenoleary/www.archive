Motive - E - Motion Theatre:

Co-directors Stephen Bain and France Hervé are experienced teachers in both France and New Zealand, where they work with students of all levels of development in the formation of choreographic Theatre, using the principles of motiv-e-motion theatre. Motiv-e-motion Theatre "Fluid emotion through the taming of motion."

Motive: Implies a principle more of less well thought out that makes us act after determination.

Motion: Quality of what is alive, what is not inert.

Emotion: Instinctive feeling as opposed to reason.

Training and Workshops:

Intensive Training

In order to reach the extremeties of our potential as actors, we must seek the limits of our natural physical and vocal abilities. The training is our ground work for all playmaking where we use improvisation work as much as technique development. Besides respiration, we channel the creative energy of every performer towards three basic principles: Direction, Objective and Precision.

Actors find a natural way to express imposed and impulsive action by focusing firstly on gestural simplicity and trust in themselves. Duets in which turns, lifts and cimbined movement enlarge the field of the actor's game possibilites. Similarly the vocal training strives for both precision and sxploration leading to an acute awareness of our natural voice, while also authorising what we call íncidents'where the sound opens new textures and resonator boundaries.

Text Based Drama

Simple dialogue is used as a starting point for the work. Actors discover the words as opportunites to 'play with the imagination', opening doors to sound and sensation without concern for character. This method is introduced through improvisation with partners, playfully encouraging exploration and the risk of discovery before any specific tasks are imposed. Through an emphasis on sponteneity and the use of space free from all character preconceptions, a simple but true connection between performers is germinated.

Further restrictions are then placed on the actors, guiding them little by little to define their motives on stage. From the physical memory of the scene a strong relations emerges which becomes the stylisation. Only from this strategic point, is the text reinvested with a naturalism. It therefore becomes the physical situation that drives the actors emotions in a spontaneous and organic way. The characterisation is finally discovered through this 'motive-e-motion' process.
