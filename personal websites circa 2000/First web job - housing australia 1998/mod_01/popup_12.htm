1. The Dominance of the Market in the Production and Allocating of Housing

The Drivers of Housing Change

10 Special Needs Groups

For much of the postwar era, housing policy in most countries was concerned with fostering production to the degree that absolute shortages were removed or slums cleared and replaced. It is really only in the last decade or so that there has been a move towards fine tuning of housing programs to meet specific needs. This direction recognises that uniform or homogeneous policies deny the diversity of needs and demands of households and individuals. It is also, in many respects, a response to an increasingly diverse demographic structure mentioned above. Groups that have come in for specific recognition include the aged, indigenous populations, the homeless and students.

The groups that get programmatic recognition, or at least debate, vary from country to country. For example, homelessness is rarely discussed in Germany and Denmark, students in Australia and Canada, native populations other than Australian Aboriginals, Canadian Inuit and New Zealand Maoris. All countries are concerned with the aged, largely because all are experiencing an aging of the population. European countries, in particular, have a high proportion of aged, as Table 5 shows.
