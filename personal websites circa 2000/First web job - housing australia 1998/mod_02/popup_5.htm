2. Home Ownership in Australia

5 Available Land and an Appropriate Finance Industry

In much of Europe, the bulk of rural and urban land was owned by a small number of enormously rich landed gentry who had little interest in subdividing it for individual ownership. Ultimately, many did convert their land for residential purposes and took on the role of private landlords, but they were reluctant to give up their land for ownership. By contrast, Australia had no landed gentry as the land was resumed from the Aboriginals who, without Western concepts of private property and a legal system to support it, were seen to have no rights to the land.

Land was purchased by people who saw it as a mechanism for quick profits. As documented by Michael Cannon (1966), a finance and development industry emerged in the 19th century which was geared to speculation in selling land for ownership. This tradition, while moderated from the excesses of that era, is still important today. The system of housing provision in Australia, consistent with the country’s market liberal status, is still geared to the provision and maintenance of ownership. Thus, the historical manner in which the finance, development and building industries were structured here has preordained Australia to be a home ownership nation.

Those who migrated to Australia wanted to own their own home, and this was matched by an industry which was interested in selling land and houses for ownership. Together with the supportive government policies since World War II, the preconditions were in place for a home owning society.

In summary, then, the dominance of ownership has no simple explanation, but rather is a confluence of features; some have their origins in the pattern of settlement in the 19th century and some reflect the characteristics of the era following World War II, including relative equality of income, full employment, high levels of migration and public policy.
