3. Social Housing Sector

4. The CSHA

As indicated above, the social housing system is funded through the CSHA, which is negotiated every three to four years. It provides grants to the States (with the States matching these grants) on the basis that they will cover capital outlays, and with the expectation that recurrent costs will be covered from revenue, principally rent. The subsidy between what tenants can afford and the actual rental (called a rebated rent) is carried as a liability of the State Housing Authorities (SHAs). Public tenants, unlike private renters, do not receive rent assistance from a central income support agency as is common in many other countries. The internal rebate is a distinctive and problematic element of the Australian system, particularly as greater targeting means that more and more households receive a rebate.

Since the early 1990s, funds under the CSHA have been contracting (see Table 3). Gross payments have fallen dramatically and will continue to fall to their lowest level for twenty years. Net CSHA funds will fall from $671 million in 1995-96 to around $500 million by 2000-2001, a fall of 25 per cent in real terms. This will see the number of dwellings added to the public housing stock decline from 5,000 in 1995-96 – itself a fifteen year low – to barely a thousand or so. From a Commonwealth government perspective, this contraction in social housing grants has been compensated by a huge growth in rent assistance to the private rental sector over the last decade and a half. Which is the best method of providing housing assistance is a contested issue which will, in all likelihood, be increasingly debated over the next decade. See C7
