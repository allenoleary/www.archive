Too High the Son

Too High the Son is inspired by the life of pioneer aviator Richard Pearse who flew for the first time in 1903 from his isolated farm in Otago, New Zealand.

The legend insinuates that he arrived ahead of the Wright brothers in the conquest for the sky. Irrefutably a self-taught genius, his work was ignored by his rural turn-of-the-century community, obliged to the demands of colonialism.

Following the relationship of three characters during the summer of 1903, Richard Pearse, his brother Warne (a farmer and inveterate tennis player), and Marie Garance ( a young French immigrant school teacher), the story reveals the determination, despite failures, of the three idealists infatuated by freedom and their struggle against uncompromising local Puritanism. Wellington Sept '96

NZ Listener 20 June '98

"Too High the Son explores the passion that drove this enigmatic figure. Stephen Bain, France Herve and Tim Spite have created a production as elusive and compelling as its subject. Bain's script sidesteps historical realism, cutting straight to the heart of Pearse's legend: the dream of flight.

Surreal and real at the same time, Too High the Son juxtaposes images of breathtaking beauty with laconic and almost painfully awkward dialogue.

Tim Spite is delightfully diffident as Warne Pearse, the down-to-earth farmer with a passion of his own for visiting teacher Marie (France Herve). Herve herself brings Marie to life with a clarity and economy that is characteristic of this production. Bain is a wonderfully distracted - or perhaps focused - Richard Pearse. As Pearse says of birds in the play: "They just live in another universe."

Dance and theatre flow seamlessly into one another in this multi-disciplinary work, creating a new language, a kind of visual poetry. Yet for all its abstract charm, Too High the Son is never pretentious or inaccessible. It is ultimately about three people and their drams, a tale told with warmth, intimacy and humour. At the same time, the play is a comment on isolation and repression, on the society from which we've come.

This is an uniquely New Zealand work that breaks through our theatre's traditional reliance on realism and on the minutae of life, claiming space for something else; for the transcendent.
Like the man who inspired it, Too High the Son has aimed for more than you'd expect, for something quite different; and this spectacular production delivers in spades."

NZ Herald 6 May '98
