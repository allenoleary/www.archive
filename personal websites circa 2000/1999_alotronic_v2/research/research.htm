"Writing with the Set; Creative Strategies for Network Writing."

This is the nominal title of my yet-to-be-tackled MA. Here's a brief description:

In the course of this MA I propose to define, research, analyse and demonstrate various creative approaches to the practice of writing under the paradigm of the Network.

I will be looking to see if there are definable features and similarities in the creative and theoretical approaches of artists to various forms of Network Writing. I will do this through the description and analysis of a range of Network pieces, working in to see if there is any common ground. I will also work out from a generative idea and make a number of Network pieces in various forms to further test any conclusions I may reach.

The works I will study are across a range of media and delivery systems, some are 'traditional' (static) and others are iterative, but all have the kind of approach I think defines 'writing the set'.

  • 'Heuretics' by Gregory Ulmer
  • 'Parc de Villette' Tschumi, Eisemann and Derrida
  • 'Iconica' Troy Innocent
  • 'Blade Runner' Westwood Software
  • 'Pale Fire' Vladimir Nabokov
  • 'The Black Sequin Dress' Jenny Kemp
  • 'Theatre of the Oppressed' by Augusto Boal

I will draw on a range of critical approaches in my description and analysis of works and approaches, from codex based textual analysis (particularly the poststructural movement), media theory, hypertext scholarship, and traditional rhetoric. I will also be looking at information theory, particularly approaches to genetic and parallel computing as well as database manipulation, which will be relevant to an understanding of Network approaches to knowledge representation.

Chora

Originally sourced from "Timeus" by Plato, the Chora is described as a third type of nature between those of being and becoming. It's a slippery concept that evokes the receptive space of creation. In Derrida's words, Chora is

"irreduclible to the two positions, the sensible and the intelligble, which have dominated the entire tradition of Western thought, it is irreducible to all the values to which we are accustomed... Chora receives everything or gives place to everything... it is absolutely blank, evreything that is printed on it is automatically effaced. "

It is a kind of reflective dimension of creative thought, a place where impossible things can be joined because nothing is represented there.

The concept of Chora interests me because it is an attempt to allow a space beyond analysis, the kind of space that Artists use routinely and yet receives little or no critical attention. Because it is beyond analysis and can only be partially apprehended though extended metaphor, it's not exactly a 'proveable' phenomenon. By switching attention to the 'network' aspect of it's being, and by using concepts of hybid space, I hope to see how it can be used as a tool/mystery in creative work for the 21st C.

The Line and the Fold

I use this oppositionary phrase to locate an ancient antagonism between various binary pairs:

    • The clean mathematical vector versus the messy emperic (Copernicus versus Keppler?)
    • The desire for pure speed versus the necessity of the organic (VR versus RL)
    • The organised and oiled versus the chaotic and dirty (Armies defeted by Guerillas)

Of course the formulation is not my own, and derives from various works by Virilio, Deleuze (in his study of the Baroque initially) and DeLanda. What great writers...

Warfare, technology and the male

I must confess to having an unhealthy interest in Warfare. Maybe because my generation has 'missed out'. Maybe because my Grandfather went. Maybe becasue I used to spend hours as a kid trying to figure out ways to play wargames by myself (the dilemnas of the only child!). Mostly I think because it's where the applcation of technology is at it's most extreme, and because some of the best books on technology I've read have been about war ('Warfare in the Age of Intelligent Machines' by Manuel De Landa is my favourite).

Also in the last decade of the last century we spent so much time watching American trying to make War clean, like a foregone conclusion, like an advertising campaign. We know the reality is extreme and costly in so many ways, mostly to the men who still have to fight them, and, of course, the 'colateral damage'. Today, more American GI's have died early from taking their own lives than lost their lives in the conflict.

My latest play 'Fond Love and Kisses' is set in NZ during WWII, and revolves around a well hidden set of events called 'the furlough incident'. A whole heap of volunteers of '39 came home for a rest in '43. Looking arounf them they saw a coutry which had no real idea of the realities of the war, they saw men making profits and taking it easy - and many of the soldiers decided not to go back to war. The Governement (ironically, the Labout Party) put a media blackout on the whole thing, and despite several protest marches, court martialled all soldiers who refused to disembark again in '44.
