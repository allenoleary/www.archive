Notes on Self Intra Network


Introduction.

This hyper-essay from the end of `96 is a response to a tutor who said `Go for the passion...' and wanted a 2.5k essay - I don't think it's possible to be passionate in that few words, so I ignored the limit... sorry David. This was also my first experience with html and hypertext.

The paper I was doing was `Textuality and Discourse' within a Graduate Diploma in Writing at Swinburne Uni (Melbourne Aust) It turned out to be a front for a course that was really about writing & the net, though we did get stuff about the history of the text/book thing.

Our tutor was pretty much pro-virt, and a lot of the reading we were doing seemed the same - I knew there had to be a problem in there somewhere...

So I found one...

The Hyper Writing Process

Having zero experience in HTML and related issues, I blithely thought a `hyper-essay' would be a quick bit of fun. I have come across the following challenges quite apart from actually writing all the material itself...

Writing without an outline.

Starting with a mind-map, I quickly sketched in all the nodes without worrying initially about how I was going to connect them. I trusted that nodes were relevant merely because they were occurring to me. I chose this way to write because I believe that a certain process will give you a certain product. If I had started with a linear essay and broken it up I would be committing a primal sin of hyper-media; that is, dressing up an old form in a new

Discovery of a linking pattern - dimensional imagination.

Having written the nodes, I mapped out the links in semantic space - what nodes had to be `close' to one another? Which nodes had to be right down the path, and which near the beginning? This design issue was fascinating and time-consuming. During this process I discovered that too-long deviations were taken when looking at secondary (`footnote') material. The way around this was to keep notes relevant to one node only `on' the node itself. For clarity, links that extended argument I keep at the exit of the node, rather than in the body text of the node itself. This ensures that the reader has to at least scan the bold selections before making their way to another page. Hopefully the reader recognises this consistency...

Rewriting and Editing.

Filling out and researching more stuff to make the nodes resonate more with each other. Because of the relatively wide range of ideas called on, I tried to keep each node orbiting in the area of one or two new concepts and explications. Most nodes have one dominant thinker as their reference.

Finding an articulatory and engaging structure.

I wanted to find a `gate' concept that was related to the material and also seductive in itself. I quickly found that just clicking around bits of text is dull - like much of the net itself. Since the subject is subject, and the subject can never wholly escape the body, I have used an initial gate of an asexual fashion illustration, itself prompted by wanting the cursor/hand to do something other than `select'. This is a weak play on `the body of the argument'. While this metaphor gets eaten up a bit, I have tried to tie it strongly in to the front and back end of the structure, and give it a little of a character...

Navigation.

One of my major beefs with the net is that often you have to idea how big a site is or how far through it you are - you can't see how many pages their are before you read it. While this is obviously sometimes the point, I decided that, given enough time, I would include the `radar' familiar to me from video games. This also gives the whole thing an implied center, a notion I play with when you reach the `heart'.

I would have explored navigation themes, maybe structuring the whole thing on a spatial semantic similar to games' N/S E/W, but with different terms on the axis - concrete/abstract, person/idea - but time and relevance were against me, and I had to settle for an outline view of the pages which you have to click into.

Production.

Like a theatre piece, the whole thing had a performance feel to the making. With the `first night' in mind (the tutor reading it), the process had a comfortable feeling to it, not unlike my days as an actor or production worker in theatre. Hand-coding HTML for Ver 1.0 of this essay was `interesting' but I prefer my editor now...

Summary

All in all I'm quite happy with the result of this first hyper-essay experience; It all felt quite natural, the theatre thing again I think.

Allen O'Leary
