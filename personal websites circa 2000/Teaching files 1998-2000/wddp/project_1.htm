Advanced Web Development Programme

Project 1: Concept and Information development

Concept and Justification, Information Architecture, Information and Visual Design.

Due: 2 weeks today (19th August)

Aims:

This project is designed to make you think about a website in a deep and analytical manner, and present your results in a clear and professional way:

  • Develop and express in writing a set of goals, rationales and user profiles for your site

  • Briefly research and analyse similar sites

  • Develop and present an appropriate information architecture for your site

  • Plan and present an information and visual design for individual pages

Requirements:

To satisfy the demands of the project you will need to complete the following tasks:

  • Analyse and write out your aims for the site

  • Be able to identify your target users and develop a site that works at their level

  • Identify competitor/peer sites and assess their strengths and weaknesses

  • Develop and present a sitemap that clearly shows the sites architecture

  • Develop information/visual designs for the various types of pages on your site.

  • Present all of the above in a concise and professional manner

Presentation:

This project will be presented in printed form. Site plans, page designs etc may be drawn on a computer or by hand. Provided the information is clear, there will be no bias towards 'designer' presentations (content, not form!). You may also be required to give a brief introduction to your project for the class - nothing too formal.

Length: I would expect that you would address a paragraph on all the major points. A good short paragraph that gets to the point will be better than a longer one that doesn't. Sometimes a sentence will be enough. Bullet points and other information strategies will be appreciated (by me and a client).

Part One: The Concept and Rationale

Going through the process of defining our goals helps us clarify what the website is actually going to do. By working through the sheets I gave you in the first week (get em here) you will have gone a long way towards defining what I consider the big questions:

  • What does this site do? (aims)

  • Who for? (users)

  • Why? (benefits)

  • How? (practical considerations)

If you don't know the answers to at least those questions, your project will be, at best, generic and unfocussed. No matter how prosaic or ordinary the answers to these questions are, they will help you remain focussed and manage your project.

Address the following points (a paragraph or key points for each):

  • A Description of the project:
    A title and brief description (what, who, why and how)

  • A Description of the Client (it may be you!):
    Their aims and expectations of the project
    Their goals as a company/individual

  • The Aims of the project:
    Three key goals of the website

  • Three important messages the website is trying to convey:

  • A description of the User:
    Who are the primary target users of the website?
    What hardware/software restrictions will they have? (Address screen size and resolution, connection speed, platforms, browser versions, plugin abilities, and special needs)

  • Describe the overall 'look and feel' of the project.
    Describe the overall visual elements and style of the project.
    Describe the overall auditory elements and style of the project (if any).
    Describe the text elements and written portions of the project.
    Describe any ideas about animation and use.

Part Two: Competitors/Peer Analysis

Find two or three websites that are competitors/peers to that of your project. For each site briefly describe the following (spend 20mins per site):

1. How successful is the site in catering for its audience? Rate it on a scale of 1-10, where 1 is very poor and 10 excellent.

2. How does the site try and reach this target audience? Describe.

3. Is the site engaging? Briefly describe.

4. Are there any special or even quirky features on the site you particularly like? Describe.

5. What do you most like about the site? Describe.

6. What do you least like? Describe.

7. Is the site interactive in any way(s)?

  • access to a database
  • search capacity
  • ability to upload files
  • e-mail available
  • forum/discussion
  • ability to download media
  • play games
  • provides live media or information
  • live chat
  • live net events
  • other

8. How would you rate the interactivity on the site on a scale of 1-10, where 1 is very poor and 10 excellent.

9. How successful is the design and navigation on the site? Rate this on a scale of 1-10, where 1 is very poor and 10 excellent.

10. Would you recommend the site to others in its target audience? Why?

11. What is the creative concept or metaphor behind the site? Describe.

12. Write a 1 or 2 sentence description of the site. Your review should be an easy-to-read summary of the site telling the reader who the site is for (its target audience), what is on it (its content) and how good you think it is (your overall opinion of it).

For example, Yahooligans reviews the Planet Girl site like this: "This online environment for girls feature games, electronic postcards and weekly serials featuring some of the coolest girl characters around."

Part Three: The Information Architecture.

Having addressed the aims of the site and looked at competitors/peers, we can start to plan how our site is going to work. Here the consideration is the progression that a user will make through the site; what pages do they see first, how is the content organised, what possible pathways exist in the site.

This should be a clear graphical presentation of a sitemap (A3 is good for this...)

Your sitemap should clearly indicate:

  • What the first page of your site is
  • What major sectional divisions exist in the information you are presenting
  • Major pathways through your site
  • The position of each page on your site in the information architecture

Part Four: Information/Visual Design

After discovering what our major divisions/pathways are, we can start to plan the information design of each of the various types of pages in the site. Here the consideration is spatial. The information design is a balance between expressing the messages of the site (look and feel, graphics etc), and allowing the user to move around easily (navigation). This balance will differ depending on the type of page that we are designing; a splash page may be all look and feel and no navigation (just a simple click-through) while a generic contents page will have less 'message', more content and navigation.

We need to match the information order with the visual order.

Carry out the following task for each type of page (splash, home, contents) in your site:

  • Make an information order for the page: what should be most important on the page? what next, etc...
  • Bearing in mind any design consideration, and taking into account colours that you will use, sketch out the layout of the page, placing elements (logo, primary navigation, secondary navigation, content) so that they reinforce the information order.
  • Label each element and note where it is on the information order.
  • Add colour to aid comprehension.
