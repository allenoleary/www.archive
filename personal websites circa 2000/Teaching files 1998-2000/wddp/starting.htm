Asking the right questions; how to start a project.

Introduction

Starting a project is often the hardest thing to do, and the first steps can make or break a project later on down the development track. How you start will largely depend on your client and the scope of the project. Often clients who 'know' most about the internet make the most incorrect assumptions about what they want; it's your role as a designer/information architect to get to grips with what they really want - you may surprise them!

Also it's important to note that in all interactions with your clients you have to talk at an appropriate level for their level of understanding, If you can manage to be informative without being patronising, then you'll have a much better change of them employing you again, or recommending you to someone else.

This kind of process also applies to any projects that you do for yourself: you may find your initial assumptions need some adjustment after you treat yourself like a client.

So, what do they really want?

Usually clients want 'a website for the company' or 'an information kiosk'. All they are talking about here is what delivery medium they want, they haven't told you anything at all about what the thing will do, how it will help them or their customers, how it will be administered and run.

At this point you should get the client to take a step backwards, and start asking the following sorts of questions (these are a selection from 'Elements of Web Design')

Conceptual development

The Big Questions:

    • What will it do?
    • Who for?
    • Why?
    • How will it do these things?

     

Goals and Messages:

  • What's the primary and secondary goals for this project?
  • What's the primary and secondary goals of the clients business overall?
  • What's the primary and secondary audience for this project? (age, sex, income, locality etc)
  • What are the audience capabilities? (How savvy are they? Net experience? How much time do they have…)
  • What is the delivery platform? (PC or Mac, internet speed. Browsers, screen size, RAM etc)
  • Top three messages the project needs to convey.

When you've established these goals and messages, take them away and think about them - then see if the goals have shifted or need shifting. Rethink and rethink!

Content

    • Does this project use existing content, or will it have to be generated?
    • In what ways will any existing content have to be re-purposed for the intended medium and audience?
    • How will new content be generated? Who will take responsibility for it?
    • Is any of the content going to be active? If so, how will it be included and who will update it?
    • Will there be any data entered by the user? Who will use it and how? Are there any privacy or legal issues?

It's very important to make an agreement with the client about who is going to supply the content and in what format. It's no good the client saying they've got all the content, only to give you a brochure and no copy in a digital format. You should charge extra for conversions into digital format (such as scanning images).

At this point you should have a pretty good idea of the size of the project, how long it might take and what kind of resources you may need (like maybe you need to call in a friend with database experience, or need to go out and find access to an important piece of equipment you may not have).

Possible Structures

From here you should check your assumptions with the client and then move forward into the information design and architecture phase, utilising your module making and linking skills to organise the content and pathways. You will also need to consider any metaphors of design that you are using (for instance, are you going to try and make the website work like a Playstation?) and how they will affect the information architecture.

Structure and Interpretation

  • What is the primary organisation scheme of the content?
  • Are there any other organisations that can be made available at the same time to help searching, browsing, learning, exploring, playing if appropriate?
  • What is the main presentation idea (metaphor) of the project?
  • What are the important and compelling features of this presentation idea?
  • Are there any novel or new features? If so, how are you going to let users know about them?

    At this point you would go through a detailed chunking and pathway process, presenting the final result as an information architecture or sitemap.

Sensorial Design

As you are working through the questions of presentation, you will probably be having ideas about how you want the project to look. You will need to establish some guiding principles for 'look and feel' with the client before you get too carried away. One way of ascertaining what a client likes is to ask them if they have any favourite sites or titles they like the look of, and get them to look at several site or titles that will be in direct competition with your project. Ultimately this might not be that important, but it will start them thinking about the look they're after and the market they're trying to appeal to.

Sensorial Design (use emotive words here!)

  • Describe the overall visual elements and style of the project.
  • Describe the overall auditory elements and style of the project.
  • Describe the text elements and written portions of the project.
  • Describe any ideas about animation and/or video style and use.
  • Is the realisation of the sensorial design within your abilities? What help might you need?

Multimedia is a collaborative process, and you will need to ascertain where the other members of your team are going to come from. If you're lucky they'll be friends or colleagues, but you may need to contract someone.

Market

Your project will be sitting with others of a similar type; they might not be competitors as such, but comparisons will be inevitable. You need to know in advance what titles or sites you are going to be compared to. This can help with the sensorial design, as you may see that there are certain cliches you need to avoid. You may need to think laterally here too - if your audience is a clubbing crowd, you may want to get around town and pick up all the related print advertising material.

Market

  • Are there any competitors for this project?
  • Who are they and what have they got right or wrong?
  • What can you learn from them? What are their strengths and weaknesses?
  • How can your product address those strengths and weaknesses?
  • Where should your project sit in relation to your competitors?

Conclusion

By the time you've got through a list of questions and tasks like this you will have a very good idea of what the product will do, how it will help the client and it's users, what it will look like. On large commercial projects the above process can take months and have it's own budget. Now you can sit down and start working on the achitecture and look and feel, ready to present your initial ideas to the client from a position of real understanding, not just a series of assumptions or guesses about what they want.
