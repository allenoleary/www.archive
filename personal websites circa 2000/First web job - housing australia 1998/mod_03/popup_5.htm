3. Social Housing Sector

5. Ongoing Reforms and Problems

In the 1990s the social housing sector was the subject of a number of reforms, due to its endemic problems, including:

  • The huge waiting list, which throughout the 1990s exceeded the number of vacant properties by a ratio of four to one (Australia-wide there are roughly 220,000 applicants on the waiting list, and only 50,000 dwellings become available each year, mainly through re-lets);
  • The dominance of pensioners and beneficiaries, with the outcome of 83 per cent of all tenants being on subsidised or rebated rents (DSS 1998). The increasing liability, combined with contracting Commonwealth outlays to the States, means a decline in SHA revenues to the degree that their financial viability is at risk; See C2
  • The aging of the stock, with the associated problems of repair and redevelopment needs. This is a problem of inadequate past maintenance, poor quality of construction during the 1950s and the sale of the best quality stock in the 1970s;
  • The concentration of much of the stock on large outer urban estates and in provincial towns (almost all in the form of detached housing) where there were limited labour market opportunities due to economic restructuring which bore disproportionately on these areas. This problem thrust to the fore issues of client choice and portability of housing subsidies so that people, in theory, could move to where the jobs are;
  • The perceived mismatch in stock, that is, small households occupying largish dwellings when more rent could be extracted if larger households were moved into such properties;
  • The horizontal inequity issue, that is, low income households in the private rental sector were perceived to get considerably less housing assistance through rent assistance than public sector households through their rebates. The argument was put that eligible public tenants received on average $3,000 of subsidy, while eligible private tenants received only $1,000. In fact, the difference is nowhere near as marked, as the average is affected by the enormously high rebates in Sydney as a result of it having the rent structures typical of global cities like London, New York or Paris. Stripped of the Sydney effect, there is a lesser horizontal inequity problem; and,
  • A perception in many quarters that the monolithic SHAs were poor housing managers, insensitive to clients’ needs, unimaginative in policy directions and broadly unaccountable.

The nature of the reforms and the principles on which they are based are outlined in C5.
