Ways of Organising Information

When working with information we usually have to discover or impose an organisational strategy. While it may seem that there are many ways to organise data, it is not the case. Organisation of data falls into 5 areas:

Location

Physical location (global, architectural), locations in a mechanism (like the body), locations in an imaginary or constructed world or schema.

Alphabet

For very large bodies of infromation, the 26 letters of the alphabet provide a familiar and age-old organisation mechanism - the index. The encyclopedia or dictionary are examples. Alpharbets are good organising mechanisms when your audience may not undertsand categories or location. Indexes are a fantastic way of finding small pieces of relevant information in a larger body - take the example of a large instruction manual, the index might be the best way to use it after a period of initial familiarisation.

Time

Time is an effective way of organising chronological events such as conventions, histories, exhibitions. A time line is a common tool to present a broad overview, as it is rapidly scanable.

Categories

Organisation by types of things. For instance the deptartment store is a category based information architecture (which closely reflects that of the home in some parts), as is www.looksmart.com. It is a great way to organise when items have a similar degree of importance. Color is particularly useful in organising categories.

Hierarchy.

When there is a need to organise from large to small, lest expensive to most, important to trivial, the hierarchy is useful. Hierarchies automatically assign value or weight to information. Graphs are often presentations of hierarchical information. Magnitude is best represented with numbers or relative units.

DataSets and Slices

Often you can 'slice' a data set in a couple of ways, providing a picture that will illuminate one particular aspect of the data. For instance I could slice the data set of the current class room in all the ways above and produce different information:

Location of birth city
Location of current house

Alphabetic indexing by last name

Time (date) of birth
Time (progrss) through course

Category of gender

Heirarchy of body weight
Heirarchy of income

The information gained could be used to see if the university was attracting equal numbers of male and female students, what age people start

Part of the beauty of the internet is that you can slice data in many different ways, often from the one page. The online YellowPages can provide more active slices than the paper version (and be more up to date).
