Aims dictate the structure

As with a building, so with a website: you have to know what you're trying to do before you can effictely structure information. This could be stated in quite a prosiac way like 'sell clothes online'. But really is that useful?

Consider Amazon.com. There mission is not to 'sell books' but to 'help readers make book buying decisions'. Quite different, and it explains completely the types of information provided on their site and the way it is structured. For instance the ability to add begative reader reviews to a page on a book doesn;t immediately seem like a great idea, but it increases trust and honesty (even if they exploit their own workers by paying crap and using stock options as a carrot!).

In the context of your project, can you come up with an aim for the site that is 'poitive' and creative like Amazons? This is the first step in good Information Architecture; knowing why you're getting people to your site in the first place...

Context and narrowing focus

When you first get someone to your site you need to contextualise them very quickly. This can mean a number of things:

  • Quickly tell them what the site does (aims)

  • Show them where they are and how to move around (navigation)

  • Appeal in a sensory and/or emotional level (design/empathy/text - start the conversation)

Further into the site, we may get more specific, by narrowing:

  • Search for just one thing

  • Read about a single item

  • Compare a list of similar items

With Amazon we can see a 'conversation'. After the initial introductions and pleasentaries, the user is invited to look through a number of categories by section. This is like a good guide asking you what sort of thing you're after first (a book, a CD), When you get to the right area, the focus narrows and you start looking through the categories, either browsing or going for something exactly. Both types of hunt are catered for (by category listings and by a search function). When you have found something interesting you get to fucus down on that, and find out what other people have thought about that book or CD or whatever. When you decide to purchase, the whole thing narrows again (to the business of extracting your credit card, address and so on). Finally you buy you're item. At any time you are free to widen your focus again and 'jump back up the tree', or try something different with a few well-guided clicks.

This might seem obvious, but it bears taking in: imagine you are actually talking and pointing users around your site: what sort of tone is appropriate at what point? When can you get technical and when generally pleasant? Good sites do exactly the same thing, they just use different methods...

Instructions

Another part of this is issuing good instructions to users. Far from being dictatorial, you really need to show people the way. In one study, people who were told to 'be creative' on a brief did better than those without the instruction. Good instructions have the following characteristics:

  • Purpose (Reason)

  • Objective (Destination)

  • Core (Procedure)

  • Time (Duration)

  • Expectation (Anticipation)

  • Failure (Error)

Consider the following:

Come to my house on Friday for dinner at 7pm. I'm having an 'end of winter' party. My address is 13 Wright St. The drive should take you 10 minutes from Kelburn. You need to go up Taranaki St until you hit Massey, then keep an eye out on your right for Monty St. Turn up it and then Wright St is first on your left. If you get to the Drama School, you've gone too far! Bring a salad and a bottle.

Then consider these:

  • You can be assited if you cannot read (Ballot paper in US)

  • Hotel Shower Cap in a box 'Fits One Head'

  • Sainsburies Peanuts: Warnng, contains Nuts

  • Xmas lights: For indoor or outdoor use only

  • You just have to be nicer to me

And Questions

One of the oldest internet traditions is the FAQ page, where most asked questions are placed together in a kind of rag-bag. This really just proves that the site doesn't work! Questions need to be asked and answered in context. The answer can always be found, but the art of asking the right question is rare. It can be a huge act of bravery just to ask a simple question and happily admit yor ignorance. You can ask clients simple direct questions that will help them and you in your work. It will save you so much time it's not funny!
