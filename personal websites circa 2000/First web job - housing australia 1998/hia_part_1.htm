A1 The Australian Housing System

Flying into any Australian city on a clear day, an international visitor is inevitably impressed by the red roofed houses sitting in detached gardens for as far as the eye can see. In Sydney the landscape might be cut by the bright blue of the harbour and its many bays, inlets and rivers, but in other cities it is a picture of almost unrelieved red and green. These sprawling cities of predominantly detached houses are probably the most visible image of what is characteristic, and occasionally unique, about Australian housing.

Behind this image is a complex housing system with many features which are different from those of other countries or, even if similar, take on different dimensions. This website takes us through the major characteristics of the Australian housing system, its policies and its problems. Let us start with a brief review of some of its most distinctive characteristics.

1 The Dominance of the Market in the Production and Allocation of Housing

In Australia, most aspects of housing provision are undertaken by the private sector. Governments do, however, have a role in providing resources for housing assistance and in establishing a framework for private sector provision, for example, planning and building regulations, taxation and other financial subsidies. Not all countries are as dependent on market provision as Australia, setting aside a larger role for government. See B6 for overview of policy.

2 The Dominance of Home Ownership

Australia has been a home owning society for longer than any other advanced industrial country. Its level of home ownership was already almost 50 per cent in the late 19th century, a figure still not achieved by most other advanced industrial countries today. While a few other nations have now achieved levels similar to the Australian average of 70 per cent over the last two decades (see Table 1), the fact remains that Australia continues to be in the forefront of countries in terms of the importance of home ownership. 4 The Dominance of the Detached House

Australian cities are some of the largest in the world in geographical area. The major reason for this is the dominance of the detached house. Most other countries, at a similar stage of economic and social development, have a greater mix of residential building forms. Australia has something of the order of 77.7 per cent detached housing, although there are variations from city to city. Link B3

5 The Relatively High Rate of New Housing Demand for an Advanced Industrial Society

Most advanced industrial societies now have relatively subdued demand for new housing. These countries have lower rates of household formation because of historically low birth rates, an aging population, and low to very little migration. Australia still maintains a relatively high rate of household formation, although the rate is contracting and may approximate the low rates of demand of other equivalent countries in a decade or so. 6 The Small-Scale and Fragmented Nature of the Building Industry

Related to the dominance of the detached house is the fact that our building industry is dominated by small-scale producers, with few building firms being national. The typical Australian firm operates in one State or region, has less than three workers, and builds about eleven dwellings per annum.

7 The Separation of Housing Development and Production

In many countries, the purchase of land, its subdivision into areas for residential construction, and the actual construction of a dwelling are undertaken by the one firm. This is relatively unusual in Australia, although becoming more common than it once was.

8 The Dominant Role of Large State Housing Authorities in Social Housing

Social housing in most advanced Western societies is provided by a mix of community organisations, housing associations, cooperatives and local government. In Australia none of these are important. The major owners and managers of social housing are the public housing agencies of each the States, with that of New South Wales being the largest provider (128,000 dwellings). Go to B

9 The Small Role of Community Housing

The corollary of the dominance of public housing provision in Australia is a small role for the community housing sector. Its origins derive from the same factors which contributed to the development of social housing generally within the Western world, that is, the activities of social reformers and philanthropic organisations concerned about the living conditions of the poor. Until the 1980s, community housing in Australia (unlike Europe) received little government sponsorship. Since then it has steadily expanded, although it is still small by overseas standards or relative to the public sector. Go to H

10 The Largish Size and Important Role of the Private Rental Sector

The private rental sector in Australia is relatively large, accounting for around 22 per cent of the housing stock. As Table 3 shows, the sector declined slowly in the postwar era but began to recover in the 1980s as home ownership became more expensive, lifestyles changed, and government policy became more supportive of the sector. By international standards the sector operates in a fairly deregulated environment, with no rent controls and fairly minimal residential tenancy controls. Distinctive attributes, besides the deregulated environment, include the large proportion of rental stock that is detached housing, the dominance of small investors (most investors own only one property) and the lack of any institutional investment. The Context of Housing Provision

The above points summarise the main characteristics of the Australian housing system. How well it works and for whom is not only a function of the system itself but of the economic, social and political context in which it operates. All countries must grapple with management of a diverse range of economic, social and environmental demands. To this effect, all countries have evolved a particular approach to the management of these demands. The Australian approach reflects our particular economic structure and social and political heritage. It is an approach which is far from universal in its principles and characteristics. Other countries have approaches which range from very similar to markedly different. This will have major repercussions for the form of housing and housing policy.

While all countries are different, many do share general attributes, and it is possible to aggregate them even if it is in a rough and ready form. One categorisation is in terms of those which are market liberal and others which are social democratic. The former share a greater emphasis on the market; they value individualism, consumer choice and self-help and have lower taxes and a smaller role of government. Labour markets and financial markets tend to be less regulated, and industry protection is lower. Australians have coined the term economic rationalist for this form of society. Australia, along with the United States and New Zealand, are market liberal or economic rationalist societies. Social democratic societies, while still market societies, place a greater emphasis on government, have higher taxes and share values of community and communitarianism.

The market liberal institutional arrangement, along with the policies and values that go with it, explains some of the distinctive features of the Australian housing system such as the low proportion of social housing and the dominance of home ownership. Among other things it is also a cause of higher levels of poverty than in social democratic societies – a factor that places considerable stress on the small social housing sector.

Another key feature of the Australian institutional structure is its federal system of governance. The federation of the colonies in 1901 created a constitutional environment with significant implications for the housing system. The federal constitution has meant that most of the laws applying to housing – for example, residential tenancy legislation, property law, building regulations and planning controls – are State or local government based, with the States protecting their rights vigorously. Unlike in the United States or Canada, there is no largish federal agency responsible for policy directions in housing, with the result that there is nothing which resembles an Australian housing policy. The Commonwealth, however, has historically had taxing powers and therefore most of the revenue. Thus, while States and local government – particularly the former – provide or set the context for housing provision, arrangements have had to be made with the Commonwealth for funding.

The Drivers of Housing Change

This website to date has sketched in outline terms a framework for understanding how any country’s housing system works. All housing systems are dynamic and constantly evolving to adapt to changing circumstances. In advanced industrial countries most are currently going through a period of change and reform. Much of the objective of this website is to provide an understanding of these changes and the implications for Australian housing. It is useful to briefly summarise the major changes shaping housing provision both in Australia and internationally if we are to understand the potentials and problems of the Australian housing system.

1 Industrial Restructuring

All advanced capitalist countries are going through a process of industrial restructuring, in order to achieve greater international competitiveness among themselves and with the emergent Asian nations. This is creating outcomes of considerable importance for housing and housing policy. A review of the major literature on industrial restructuring and an analysis of empirical trends over the last decade would indicate two important outcomes:

  • Relatively higher levels of unemployment for longer time periods, compared to earlier decades of the postwar era; and,
  • Substantial increases in casual labour, both because of the need for a more flexible workforce and because a number of the growth industries (for example, services and tourism) are characterised by a dependence on casual labour.

Both outcomes suggest that a substantial minority of households in most countries will be on very low to low incomes, with important implications for housing need and demand. Unless there are significant income support programs to top up low labour market incomes, signs point to considerably larger numbers in the 21st century who will be unable to afford private market provided housing as we know it. This may produce new private initiatives to keep housing affordable (for example, more multi-unit housing), new forms of adaptive behaviour by lower income households (for example, shared living arrangements) or new forms of housing assistance.

2 Social and Spatial Polarisation

One of the unintended outcomes of spatial restructuring in the interests of efficiency and competitiveness is what is increasingly called social and spatial polarisation. This term captures the notion that while the benefits of restructuring may flow to many – perhaps most – households, a sizable minority will be bypassed. There will therefore be greater polarisation between the affluent and non-affluent; in the absence of countervailing policies, this will increasingly take on a spatial dimension. The concern by some is that the affluent will reside in certain parts of cities and in certain regions, sometimes in gated and walled communities, and those less well off in other increasingly resource-deprived and unsustainable communities. How to create sustainable communities that minimise crime, violence, welfare dependency and family breakdown will be a major housing and planning challenge in coming decades.

3 Efficiency

Driven by the requirements of industrial restructuring, perceived problems of government debt and – more recently – international recession, governments over the last decade have become more concerned with economic efficiency than during the ‘long boom’ years of the postwar era. This is particularly the case with the more market liberal societies such as Australia. Principles encompassed by the term efficiency which have relevance for housing policy include:

  • Privatisation, which is premised on the assumption that private provision is more efficient than government provision;
  • Targeting, which requires identification of those in greatest need and targeting resources specifically to such groups;
  • Market discipline, which assumes that market prices will produce better decision making by providing a measure of monetary discipline;
  • Visibility of subsidies, which requires that any subsidy be easily identified and costed, thereby facilitating financial accountability and responsibility;
  • Client accountability/consumer sovereignty, which captures the idea that clients or consumers know best their specific needs, thereby preventing inefficiencies created by bureaucratic determination of need; and,
    •
  • Externalities, which captures the idea that private and government actions have unanticipated costs and benefits for the wider economy and society, and thereby measures of efficiency extend beyond narrow accounting measures of costs.

4 Management

For various reasons, the non-private housing stock in all countries up until the late 1970s was managed in a rather bureaucratic style, even where cooperatives were a large part of the stock. Subsequently there has been a push towards management reforms. While the objectives may be the same – to make decision making more client sensitive, to empower clients and to create greater efficiency – the balance between these objectives and the appropriate reforms is different from country to country. In Australia it has seen a fostering of community housing as an alternative management approach to public providers.

The push for better management is not just a public concern. Private renters have not necessarily been served well in the private rental sector, with estate agencies treating it as a side business to selling. In recent years there has been a push towards more professional management of this sector.

5 Urban Form

A concern for urban form has waxed and waned over the postwar years. It is now very much back on the agenda, with housing policy being seen as intrinsic to achieving a more efficient, equitable and environmentally sustainable urban form. In Canada, New Zealand and Australia, the concern is with urban consolidation, while in Europe and the United States it is very much linked with inner urban revival (for example, the United Kingdom and the United States) or urban conservation (for example, Denmark and Germany).

7 Household Diversity

While there may be differences in household growth between countries, all share a tendency to household diversity. The stereotypical nuclear family is becoming less important, and housing now has to meet the needs of single parents, singles, group households and childless couples. Moreover, diversity is being further created by greater ethnic and lifestyle differences in a multicultural society such as Australia.

8 Affordability

Most advanced industrial countries have experienced eroding affordability over the last decade, and the provision of affordable housing is a major objective. Unfortunately, the articulation of an objective does not ensure success in addressing it, as few countries appear to have made major inroads into improving affordability despite major policy reforms. However, any discussion of affordability problems requires some specification of the affordability concept and what a problem represents. Thus, in Australia, we may complacently accept that housing costs, on average, only consume 12.6 per cent of household income and therefore conclude that there is no affordability problem. The issue in Australia, as in all advanced industrial nations, is not whether there is a general affordability problem, but whether there is one for specific groups and whether the trend is towards continued erosion of affordability for such groups.

Affordability is principally a problem for newly forming households beginning their housing careers, for single income households (mainly single parents and single persons) and for groups who, for whatever reason, have low incomes. Typically, the measure of housing affordability is some yardstick criteria of housing costs as a proportion of income, such as not more than 20, 25 or 30 per cent. The greater the number exceeding the yardstick, the greater the affordability problem. The evidence from virtually all countries reviewed shows that the numbers experiencing such a problem are growing. Housing policy reform is increasingly being shaped by an awareness of this.

However, the degree to which housing affordability is being experienced as a problem depends on the existing character of the housing market, the form and degree of government intervention (including comprehensiveness of welfare programs) and the state of the economy. It would appear that affordability is more of a problem in countries with a small social housing sector – and, conversely, private market dependence – and leaner welfare systems. Thus, the problem appears less intense in Germany and Denmark.

9 Poverty and Low Income

Any discussion of low income housing policy and affordability must give some attention to the incidence of low income and its most extreme manifestation, poverty, as well as the determinants of this incidence. This is not an easy task as there are difficulties both in defining income and attributing causal connections. Direct income for an individual or household is derived from participation in the formal labour market or, if outside the market, by income support from the state, for example, pensions and benefits.

Indirectly, income can be determined by the degree and form of the social wage, that is, goods and services provided free of charge or at subsidised prices which increase a person’s or household’s disposable income relative to consumption of these goods and services at market values. Education, health care, child care and public transport are characteristic of social wage goods. The cumulative effects of labour market income, income support and the social wage can create very different income positions between societies.

Market liberal societies such as the United States and Australia, with their relatively unregulated and flexible labour markets, can have people working at much lower incomes for a given position than more regulated and centralised markets such as the social democratic societies of Europe. Moreover, income support benefits tend to be lower, so there is greater poverty. Thus, the proportion of persons and households requiring low income housing assistance may be greater in market liberal societies. How this is handled in policy terms is a complex issue as there are two broad means: to subsidise people into private rental housing through rent assistance, or to construct social housing and subsidise people by allocating them such housing. Which is best has long been debated, but Australia is currently moving from the latter to the former.

10 Special Needs Groups

For much of the postwar era, housing policy in most countries was concerned with fostering production to the degree that absolute shortages were removed or slums cleared and replaced. It is really only in the last decade or so that there has been a move towards fine tuning of housing programs to meet specific needs. This direction recognises that uniform or homogeneous policies deny the diversity of needs and demands of households and individuals. It is also, in many respects, a response to an increasingly diverse demographic structure mentioned above. Groups that have come in for specific recognition include the aged, indigenous populations, the homeless and students.

The groups that get programmatic recognition, or at least debate, vary from country to country. For example, homelessness is rarely discussed in Germany and Denmark, students in Australia and Canada, native populations other than Australian Aboriginals, Canadian Inuit and New Zealand Maoris. All countries are concerned with the aged, largely because all are experiencing an aging of the population. European countries, in particular, have a high proportion of aged, as Table 5 shows.

11 Direct Provision versus Indirect Provision: People or Property?

In part because of the need to create greater public sector efficiency in a context of industrial restructuring and globalisation, and in part because of factors specific to each country, the last decade or so has seen considerable debate and policy changes around the issue of whether to subsidise people or property. The provision of adequate and affordable housing will always require subsidy for many households. The private sector simply cannot price a product to provide affordability for all households.

But what is the most efficient form of subsidy? Is it in the form of directly providing subsidised social housing to households or is it in the form of some financial assistance or supplement to enable them to shop around for their housing, for example, Australia’s rent assistance. While Australia has made something of a transition from direct to indirect assistance in the last two decades, the debate as to where to go in the future is continuing. For more details on the rationale for the two models of assistance, go to C7.

1 Income Levels and Ownership

One way of addressing this question is to dismiss some popular explanations. Particularly in the 1960s and 1970s, one common view was that home ownership was a measure of Australia’s affluence and that, as other countries became richer, they would emulate this preference. 2 A Natural Tenure?

A second argument is that home ownership is somehow a natural tenure form, reflecting an innate desire of people to own, as well as recognition that it is an innately superior tenure. The above data also, in part, invalidates this argument. If home ownership were natural, then all countries would gravitate to it as income rose. Poverty, in this context, is seen as the factor which blocks people’s natural inclination to ownership. The more important argument, as expressed by Kemeny (1983), is that there is nothing innately superior in home ownership. It only takes on that appearance in the context of the financial and legal support available for this tenure relative to others. Thus, what are the perceived attractions of home ownership that could make it the natural choice of households?

Most people are attracted to ownership because it is secure (a home of your own, offering freedom to renovate and decorate), is cheaper over a lifetime, and is an investment. In Australia most would agree that only ownership offers these characteristics. But in some countries, residential tenancy legislation allows the tenant security for life and, indeed, allows for a rental property to be passed on to children. Similar legislation offers tenants the right to renovate and decorate. In many countries, a different set of financial structures and subsidies means that ownership is often more expensive than other tenure types. Further, the decline in new housing demand as the population ages and the reduction in subsidies for ownership have removed the tendency for house price inflation and capital gain. In many European countries, particularly the United Kingdom, home ownership has become a financial millstone for households who have mortgages in excess of the worth of their property.

This is not to undermine the importance of home ownership, but simply to point out that its success is dependent on the institutional context. Australia has, for various reasons, always had a context which is conducive to ownership.

A popular line of argument in the 1970s and 1980s, which was given considerable prominence by Kemeny’s influential book, was that the desire for ownership is a response to government policy. He and others documented the many postwar policies which encouraged ownership and, by contrast, the relative absence of support for public and private rental. Regulated low mortgage interest rates, subsidies to first homebuyers and the absence of capital gains tax have all been identified as factors encouraging ownership. There is no doubt that such policies had their impact in reinforcing ownership in the postwar era, but they fail to explain why home ownership was important in earlier years when it had little government support.

The success of one tenure cannot be considered in isolation from others. If another tenure is perceived to be more attractive than home ownership for various reasons, then ownership may not prosper. This has not been the case in Australia. One reason for the lesser importance of ownership in many European societies has been the growth of social housing. With the size of this sector going up to around 40 per cent in, for example, the Netherlands and Sweden, home ownership by comparison just has to be squeezed out. In Australia, where political decisions have kept the social sector small, there was no threat to home ownership. This is of indirect importance to ownership, but still does not explain its dominance in the 20th century.

4 Migration

Australia is a country of migrants. The indigenous Aboriginals and Torres Strait Islanders have been swamped by successive generations of migrants, mainly from poor working-class backgrounds. Most came from large industrial cities where they lived in rental housing which gave them little security. Upon arrival in Australia, a housing sector that promised some control over their own destiny must have had immense allure. It is important in this context to note that, within one or two generations, most ethnic groups achieve ownership rates higher than the longer established population.

5 Available Land and an Appropriate Finance Industry

In much of Europe, the bulk of rural and urban land was owned by a small number of enormously rich landed gentry who had little interest in subdividing it for individual ownership. Ultimately, many did convert their land for residential purposes and took on the role of private landlords, but they were reluctant to give up their land for ownership. By contrast, Australia had no landed gentry as the land was resumed from the Aboriginals who, without Western concepts of private property and a legal system to support it, were seen to have no rights to the land.

Land was purchased by people who saw it as a mechanism for quick profits. As documented by Michael Cannon (1966), a finance and development industry emerged in the 19th century which was geared to speculation in selling land for ownership. This tradition, while moderated from the excesses of that era, is still important today. The system of housing provision in Australia, consistent with the country’s market liberal status, is still geared to the provision and maintenance of ownership. Thus, the historical manner in which the finance, development and building industries were structured here has preordained Australia to be a home ownership nation.

Those who migrated to Australia wanted to own their own home, and this was matched by an industry which was interested in selling land and houses for ownership. Together with the supportive government policies since World War II, the preconditions were in place for a home owning society.

In summary, then, the dominance of ownership has no simple explanation, but rather is a confluence of features; some have their origins in the pattern of settlement in the 19th century and some reflect the characteristics of the era following World War II, including relative equality of income, full employment, high levels of migration and public policy.

B2 Social Housing

The Australian social housing sector has few parallels internationally. Reflecting the particular characteristics of our cities, our often confused and dichotomous values (a market liberal society with a strong sense of fairness) and the nature of the federal system (strong central and State government, and weak local government), Australia created in the immediate postwar era a highly centralised public housing sector.

Whereas in most countries social housing systems evolved around the community sector or local government, that is, with some reference to locality and local identity, in Australia they did not. The system was built up around eight large State and Territory housing providers, funded largely by the Commonwealth government through the Commonwealth-State Housing Agreement (CSHA). Local government has had a minimal role in public provision. In total, around 5 per cent of the stock is social housing (see Table 1).

The community sector, although now around for at least two decades, manages only about 0.2 per cent of this stock. The sector had a late start compared to many other countries and is still very small, but it is now the growth component of social housing, as a result of a conscious decision by governments to provide greater choice within the system. Another major characteristic of the Australian social housing system is the type of stock. Where in most countries this is multi-unit housing, often high rise, the bulk of the Australian stock is detached housing, much of it on broad acre estates in the middle ring suburbs and outer areas of the cities. There is some high rise, mainly in the inner areas of Melbourne and Sydney, but such estates do not have the same problems as equivalent stock overseas.

Other distinctive aspects of the Australian social housing system include:

  • The subsidy to households in the form of a rebate on rents provided by the housing agencies themselves, rather than a rent supplement from some central government agency; Link C2
  • Market rent, not cost rent; Link C3
  • An allocation system increasingly targeted at special needs, rather than low income or inability to access private rental; Link C4
  • The emergence of a national social housing reform agenda in the 1990s; Link C5 and,

The small size of the sector, along with its other distinctive features, has created a complex and challenging environment for social housing workers, discussed in more detail in C8.

As indicated above, the social housing system is funded through the CSHA, which is negotiated every three to four years. It provides grants to the States (with the States matching these grants) on the basis that they will cover capital outlays, and with the expectation that recurrent costs will be covered from revenue, principally rent. The subsidy between what tenants can afford and the actual rental (called a rebated rent) is carried as a liability of the State Housing Authorities (SHAs). Public tenants, unlike private renters, do not receive rent assistance from a central income support agency as is common in many other countries. The internal rebate is a distinctive and problematic element of the Australian system, particularly as greater targeting means that more and more households receive a rebate.

The reason why Australia has disproportionately built detached housing, when most other countries have built mainly medium density, has not been adequately researched. It can be argued that it is a response to the system of provision that has existed since white colonisation, which includes the following factors:

  • The long-time existence of a small-scale building industry, often owner built, with little expertise or few resources for constructing other housing forms;
  • The availability of cheap and accessible land, which meant that there was little pressure to develop alternative housing forms;
  • The dominance of home ownership, with the corollary of a smaller rental sector. Landlords prefer medium density housing as it is easier and cheaper to manage than detached housing. The absence of the large private rental sector of other countries meant that housing was commissioned for and by individual households rather than large landlords;
  • A rapidly growing population of migrants, mainly from dense inner city tenements and terraces, who no doubt saw the detached house as symbolising a break with their unpleasant pasts;
  • A facilitative planning system characteristic of a market liberal society that enabled – indeed, encouraged – urban sprawl, a form of urban development that fits the detached house; and,
  • Pressure on local government to enact planning controls and covenants that prevented housing other than the detached house from being developed in many parts of our cities. This restricted medium density housing to certain areas, mainly the inner city with its already established dense housing form.

The large size of the Australian house and the allotment on which it is located means that our cities are very low density. Table 2 compares the densities of world cities. Australian cities are down at the bottom of the scale, with 18 or fewer persons per hectare. Hong Kong, by contrast, has almost 300 persons per hectare. Such low densities are problematic, particularly for lower income earners. Public transport services in cities below 30 persons per hectare are rarely cost effective and often have limited routes and timetables. This restricts the mobility of the poor, the disabled, single parents and young or aged persons, that is, those who are less likely to be car owners. It also tends to imply that the cities are more polluted from car exhaust fumes, and very dependent on cheap and available energy sources.

Medium density housing has become the general term to capture a range of dwelling types including terraces, flats, townhouses and small lot subdivision housing. The term really conflates two different types of housing development, that is, multi-unit housing such as apartments, and developments of detached and attached dwellings at higher than normal densities.

The peak period for its construction in Australia was 1965-75, mainly in the form of blocks of flats, or what are referred to in Queensland as ‘six packs’, given their approximation to a six pack of beer. For a variety of reasons, this design fell into decline and has only staged a resurgence in recent years. This resurgence is mainly an inner urban one, driven by changing lifestyles and demographics and by the economic reality for some households that the single detached house, at least in inner urban areas, is not affordable.

It is not only consumers who are discovering the merits of medium density housing. All levels of government, for a combination of economic and environmental reasons, have come to appreciate that our existing pattern of low density development must be modified. In particular, recognition of the high costs of infrastructure provision on the urban fringe has encouraged a view that greater use should be made of existing urban infrastructure. The inner areas of our cities are seen as having considerable potential for urban consolidation. There are many pockets of underutilised land (although these are rapidly being depleted) as a result of changing economic activity, such as the relocation of manufacturing and warehousing to outer urban areas.

The process of urban consolidation, which at the moment is essentially a policy of medium density housing, is an inevitable response to a local, national and world environment which is very different to that of previous decades. Unfortunately, it is often difficult for people immersed in the demands of their daily lives to appreciate that we have entered a new economic, social and urban order – an order increasingly called the postmodernist world to distinguish it from the modern world of sustained economic growth and expansive government that characterised the postwar years up to the 1970s.

Many people still believe that what held for the past will hold for the future, and therefore our cities should continue as they have done for decades. This denies that the purchase costs of housing today, despite relatively low interest rates, are of a very different order to the period from 1950 through to the early 1980s; that high unemployment will be with us for the foreseeable future; that governments no longer have the discretionary funds for urban expansion; that the demographic structure and, therefore, housing needs are very different to those of past decades; and that Australian industry is going through a marked restructuring which has all sorts of income and employment implications.

The postmodernist Australia is simply one which will not enable the old urban form, based on the assumption of the detached house for all families, to be sustained to the degree of the past. This is not to suggest that urban consolidation is a universal and comprehensive panacea for Australia’s urban and economic problems. There are major problems with consolidation, and the reality is that the detached house on the fringe will continue to provide the bulk of new housing. Housing provision cannot be ahead of consumer preferences, and these are not changing so rapidly that the present increase in medium density housing can be heralded as a revolution which will see the end of the detached house.

B5 Housing Policy

There are two starting points for understanding housing policy and program delivery in Australia. The first is the recognition that Australia is a federal system, with different policy roles and responsibilities for the three levels of government. The second is the appreciation that many policies other than housing policies have important housing outcomes, for example, taxation policy.

The Commonwealth government provides much of the finance for social housing assistance and, in conjunction with the States, sets the parameters for delivery of social housing programs through the Commonwealth-State Housing Agreement (CSHA). The Commonwealth also provides rent assistance for private renters. It is also important in terms of indirect policy, notably, taxation policy.

State governments manage and deliver public housing and set the framework for the operations of the community sector, and to a large extent fund that sector. They also collect land tax and stamp duty on property sales, have responsibility for residential tenancy legislation, and establish the broad urban and regional planning framework in which local government operates.

Local governments have a very limited role in actual housing provision (unlike in many other countries) and largely concern themselves with land use planning, particularly in the control of estate development and building permits. These can have important indirect outcomes on the form and affordability of dwellings.

The major housing policies and programs are outlined below.

1 Commonwealth-State Housing Agreement

The CSHA is an agreement between the Commonwealth and each State and Territory government for the provision of certain forms of housing assistance. These are:

  • Public housing (the bulk of the funds);
  • Community housing;
  • Home purchase assistance (minimal compared to the past); and,
  • Housing assistance for special groups such as Aboriginals and Torres Strait Islanders.

The funds provided by the Commonwealth are matched by the States and Territories. Funding through the CSHA to the States over recent years has declined in real terms, although this must be put in the context of greatly expanded outlays on rent assistance by the Commonwealth, so that overall levels of housing assistance have been maintained.

The CSHA has been around since 1946 and is renegotiated every three to four years, with the terms and conditions varying greatly over time in the discretion that it allows the States and in policy emphasis. Some grants within any of the CSHAs have been tied to special purpose programs in order to ensure that the States actually commit funds to these areas. These have included pensioner rental housing programs, Aboriginal rental housing programs, community housing and crisis accommodation. Funds for public housing have generally been untied, although there are requirements for performance, data collection and planning and policy. The most recent CSHAs (1989 onwards) have been multilateral agreements, with the same conditions required of all States and Territories, although the CSHA for the period 1999-2003 is a bilateral agreement between the Commonwealth and individual States which allows for greater discretion for States to chart different housing assistance courses.

2 Rent Assistance

For most of the postwar era, public housing through the CSHA was the major form of housing assistance. A form of rent assistance called supplementary assistance was first introduced in 1958 but with very limited eligibility, largely widows and single aged pensioners, and at the discretion of the Commonwealth Department of Social Service (DSS). Its limited eligibility is reflected in the fact that in 1963 there were only 9,518 recipients. At the time, this approach was seen as a cheaper option than a more general pension increase. With some widening of eligibility, this remained in place until 1983 when the newly elected Labor government expanded the scheme to include almost all DSS pensioners and beneficiaries, as well as making it an entitlement rather than a discretionary scheme. Rent assistance is not designed to reduce rental outlays to some benchmark, for example, 25 per cent of income, but simply to improve housing affordability for social security recipients. To receive rent assistance, clients must be paying a rent above some minimum threshold per week which varies depending on household type, with the rate of payment being 75 cents in the dollar until a maximum is reached. Without it, 77 per cent of social security income units would be paying more than 30 per cent of their income in rent, but with it the figure is reduced to 39 per cent; 9 percent still pay more than 50 per cent, even with rent assistance (DSS 1998: section 5.2).

With the 1980s coinciding with a growth in numbers of aged pensioners and supporting parents and, towards the end of that period, a sharp increase in unemployment, the numbers of recipients expanded almost exponentially. In March 1998 average rent assistance per week was $29.73, with some 900,000 recipient individuals or households. In 1997-98 it was estimated that the total outlays of the rent assistance program were $1.6 billion, compared to $420 million in 1984. In real terms this represents a fourfold increase (DSS 1998: section 5.2).

Effectively, private rent assistance has now replaced public housing as the major funding platform for low income housing assistance in Australia. However, because it is not overly generous, many recipients are still likely to be in or near poverty even after its receipt. Importantly, it has more of the characteristics of an income support program than a rent assistance program and, unlike the programs of Canada, the United States (Section 8) and most Northern European countries, there is no link to any supply side subsidies. Despite rent assistance, poverty after meeting housing costs has increased in Australia from the mid-1970s, with the largest number still in the private rental sector.

The shift from social housing expenditure reflects a philosophical change in how we view the best way of providing housing assistance. It is argued that bricks and mortar investment, as typified by public and community housing, requires bureaucratic allocation of housing and ongoing control over the lives of households. By contrast, rent assistance gives households the ability to choose accommodation which is consistent with their needs. Moreover, for any given budgetary allocation, for example, $1 million, a much greater number of households can be assisted through rent assistance than through social housing investment. See C7 for more on this topic.

These arguments sound compelling but, as with all policy issues, the reality is more complex. Over time, there is ultimately some point where the cumulative effect of investment in actual dwellings (by virtue of the sheer amount of stock constructed) assists more people than rent assistance. Similarly, there are issues that the availability of rent assistance cannot address, for example, shortages of affordable accommodation in certain locations, discrimination, housing quality, and ability to link recipients with support services as in social housing.

The future of the next CSHA will depend on whether social housing can demonstrate that its benefits (including any non-shelter benefits such as family maintenance, employment and educational opportunity, and sustainable communities) exceed the benefits (including non-shelter benefits) from rent assistance or some equivalent private rental program.

3 Home Purchase Assistance

Australia has had a tradition of home ownership longer than virtually any other advanced industrial country and, at least in the first four postwar decades, the dream and reality of ownership were underpinned by a raft of home purchase assistance programs. Today there is little direct assistance, although the introduction of a GST in July 2000 was accompanied by a compensatory $7,500 grant for first homebuyers. The major forms of assistance are indirect, notably, exemption from capital gains tax for the principal residence and the absence of imputed rental income arising from ownership. There is also a small, highly targeted home purchase and mortgage assistance program within the CSHA, but this is only around $30 million spread across all the States and Territories.

4 Private Rental Tax Assistance

A tax provision called negative gearing allows landlords to claim any loss from rental operations (including costs of interest payments) as an offset to other earnings. This is an untargeted program and, unlike in many other countries, is not related to achieving any specific housing outcomes such as affordability, quality or location. Newly constructed private rental housing is eligible for a 2.5 per cent building allowance.

5 Homelessness Policies and Programs

For a variety of reasons, homelessness has increased substantially in recent decades but, unlike many other countries, Australia does have a comprehensive and nationally coordinated program to address the problem, if not solve it. The Supported Accommodation Assistance Program (SAAP) is a Commonwealth funded, but State and Territory administered, program which provides recurrent funding for salaries and other operational costs associated with housing and support assistance to homeless people and those in crisis. The Crisis Accommodation Program (CAP) provides the capital funding for dwellings for those in housing crisis.

SAAP agencies focus on six main groups: young people, single men, single women, families, women escaping domestic violence, and cross-target or multiple target groups. Of these, the two accounting for the bulk of funds are young people and women escaping domestic violence. In recent years, spending on SAAP and CAP programs has exceeded $250 million, with the bulk of funds (around $200 million) in the SAAP component. A national data collection process has been established for SAAP to provide comprehensive information on who is presenting for homelessness assistance and the reasons for their crisis situation. This data is collected by the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare.

An Australian housing worker’s role is a complex, difficult and rewarding one. They need to be a social worker, a tenant manager, a property manager, a team worker, a networker, a financial manager and a negotiator. The degree to which any of these skills is used depends on the particular position and the type of housing agency in which they work, but all these roles are required at some stage, and many at the same time.

The tasks which dictate these multiple roles derive in part from the highly targeted nature of the social housing system where virtually all tenants are poor and most suffer from some form of disadvantage, whether that be unemployment, domestic violence, drug and alcohol addiction, disability, AIDS or homelessness. But it also relates to the small size of the stock compared to many other countries and, in many cases, its condition. The small size means that matching housing needs to an actual dwelling is difficult, more so if it is the wrong size or in poor condition.

What are the key tasks of a housing worker?:

  • Determining eligibility and managing allocations;
  • Managing the rent assistance process;
  • Assisting tenants to maintain their tenancy;
  • Managing rent collection and arrears;
  • Administration of property, including managing vacancies, responding to maintenance and repair, and ensuring effective utilisation;
  • Representing the organisation in court;
  • Helping to create a comfortable work environment; and,
  • Assisting in managing the financial position of the organisation.

A review of these tasks quickly indicates that they require elements of all the roles outlined above. This is best understood by having a closer look at the tasks.

1 Determining Eligibility and Managing Allocations

This is a tenant management role, but also a social work and networking role. A potential applicant will be interviewed for eligibility, but this seemingly technical task involves much more than assessing their attributes against eligibility criteria. The ‘social worker’ role may require sensitively drawing out whether the person has problems which mean that they may not be able to sustain a tenancy without some form of ongoing support. If this is the case, when the person is allocated a property the housing worker will have to liaise with other agencies to try and obtain the necessary level of support – this requires networking and negotiation skills. Each agency has broadly similar eligibility requirements of residency, income assets and documentation of special needs. For more information on the principles of eligibility and allocation and the difficulties in administering an allocations system, go to C4.

2 Managing the Rent assistance Process

This is the process of determining what level of rent a tenant should pay, and advising them of their obligations re payments, for example, due dates, and notification of changes in income and household arrangements that might affect rent payments. In the State sector, tenants pay a market rent and receive a rebate to keep levels of rent affordable. In the community sector it is more complicated, reflecting the different forms of community housing (see C7). In some cases, tenants can get a rebated rent and receive rent assistance; in other cases, they only get a rebated rent. Most community housing agencies change a market rent, but some charge a cost rent. For more on issues of rent rebate, go to C2, and rent setting, go to C3.

3 Assisting Tenants to Maintain Their Tenancy

Allocating properties and establishing the appropriate level of assistance, for example, 23 or 25 per cent of income, is only the starting point for managing a tenancy. The challenge then is to assist tenants to maintain their tenancy. This may involve:

  • Explaining their rights and obligations, that is, the nature of the tenancy agreement;
  • Assisting the completion of the tenancy agreement;
  • Recognising that the tenant has more than just a need for housing, and may have a problem which requires support or early intervention;
  • Arranging support;
  • Monitoring rent payments, neighbourhood disputes and care of property, and intervening where necessary;
  • Taking appropriate action in response to complaints; and,
  • Facilitating appeals.

This list disguises a complex management task which can involve the housing worker in an often emotional and demanding relationship with clients. C10 looks at this relationship in more detail and covers the sort of problems that any housing worker is likely to experience.

4 Managing Rent Collection and Arrears

Collecting rents is one of the key tenant management tasks. Rents are a vital source of the finances of any landlord, whether social or private, and it is important that tenants keep up their rents. For most clients this is a relatively simple administrative task, particularly when they agree to direct debiting. For a variety of reasons, some fall into arrears, and this often requires social work and negotiation skills to resolve a satisfactory outcome. Some may think that the simple solution is to evict the tenant but, like all aspects of social housing administration, the reality is more complex and eviction should be a last resort. For more information on arrears administration issues, go to C9.

As a housing worker in these areas you should be confidently able to:

  • Explain and implement your organisation’s policy and procedures on arrears;
  • Understand and manage the arrears process;
  • Monitor a client’s payment history;
  • Recognise, analyse and/or discover the circumstances which are causing arrears; and,
  • Initiate arrears recovery action in an appropriate way.

5 Administration of Property

A housing worker has responsibility for managing a certain housing stock. This is the asset base of the housing organisation, whose long-term viability depends on how well the asset is managed. An asset gets it worth from:

  • Its location. Premium locations cost more and this is a problem for housing agencies. How do you provide and maintain stock in expensive areas?;
  • Its size. A three-bedroom dwelling is a more expensive asset than a two-bedroom one, but may yield a higher rental return than a one-bedroom. Yet need may be for a one-bedroom property. How do you balance divergent asset demands?;
  • Its quality. Quality building materials and fittings cost more but may last longer; and,
  • Its design. There are a myriad of potential architectural styles and layouts. Some may have long-term asset management costs, for example, trellis work that requires expensive maintenance.

Most of any social housing agency’s stock is old – sometimes very old – and the attributes of location, size, quality and design are visible symbols of past decisions. This may mean that you are managing stock that is below contemporary standards, is in the wrong location and wrong size relative to need, or is simply wearing out and requiring a lot of maintenance. There is not much that an individual housing worker can do about these problems, but you do have to:

  • Try to minimise vacancies by getting tenants into dwellings, even if they are not the right size, location or design;
  • Assess the property’s condition and where possible organise the necessary work to make it suitable for habitation;
  • Manage the mismatch between stock and tenant need as well as possible. This often means that allocations policy becomes an important asset management policy. The wrong person in the wrong house or street can potentially precipitate property damage and may be a source of neighbourhood nuisance;
  • Respond to property maintenance inquiries by clearly establishing the nature and type of inquiry (describing some maintenance problems to a housing worker, like illnesses to a doctor, is not easy);
  • Research the validity of the inquiry and identify an appropriate response;
  • Arrange for maintenance and repairs with the appropriate contractor;
  • Follow up with clients to see that the job was done well; and,
  • Update the asset status of the dwelling so that subsequent workers and management will know the condition status of all stock.

C10 The Worker/Client Relationship

Creating a culture of client service in social housing is difficult, particularly at the level of worker/client relations. Tensions clearly exist between them, which is understandable given the context of their interaction, but if these are identified and examined this can provide the basis for improvements in service delivery, to the benefit of all. What are these tensions and where do they derive from?

1 Impact of Wider Social Values

Some of the problem is that workers transfer the values of the wider society to their jobs. The principle manifestation is the ‘blaming the victim’ ideology, whereby the problems of social housing tenants are made out to be caused by the tenants themselves (that is, drinking, gambling and work avoidance), rather than by the structural problem that the private market simply cannot provide affordable housing for all, irrespective of an individual’s or household’s personal attributes. This ideology is entrenched quite deeply in Australia, so it is not surprising that some housing workers bring the same values to their work setting and treat households as if they were the ‘undeserving poor’.

Similarly, in the current climate of ‘commercialisation’ and ‘efficiency’, workers can too easily be seduced by this language and culture. In issues of stock management, for example, it is clear that many workers have come to accept that social housing tenants underoccupying a dwelling represent an inefficiency and should be forced to move to a more appropriate dwelling. This implies that social housing tenants do not have the right to see their house as a home, as a source of their identity and a repository of memories and sentiments, in the way that residents in other tenure sectors do. Such beliefs are anathema to quality client service. Thus, in an organisational climate where tenants are seen as burdens on the public purse, it is not surprising that many workers have evolved mixed attitudes towards clients, who in return have developed a suspicion of the workers.

One of the rationales for the community sector has been that it is more tenant focused, in effect, more client oriented. But even tenant cooperatives have not been insulated from the concept of the ‘deserving poor’, with the tenant sometimes suspicious of the attitudes and behaviours of fellow tenants, and the occasional adoption of allocations practices which exclude households that do not share the participatory values of the cooperative.

2 Budget Constraints

No organisation has unlimited resources, and some are more limited than others in their resource capacity. Human service organisations such as housing agencies cannot supplement their income from private market activities, for example, by selling their product at a profit. Workers therefore operate in a financial context where there is often simply not enough staff to provide a quality service. Requiring workers to increase output and quality without matching resources can create tensions which flow over to clients.

3 Organisational Structure

Historically, the bulk of low income housing delivery in Australia has been by large public housing authorities. The more diverse and normally smaller housing organisations that characterise most other countries have been lacking, with the exception of a very small cooperative sector. For much of the postwar era, these large organisations were highly centralised in their management structure. The steps to regionalise by devolving delivery to a more local level or by establishing a viable community sector only really occurred in the last decade. This means that there has existed a culture of delivery which tends to be remote from the clients and their needs. Changing that culture will not occur overnight.

Largish organisations must develop complex communication systems to ensure that workers know what to do and why. The larger the organisation, the more intermediaries – either persons or memos or reports – through which a message is filtered from senior management to the coalface. Two problems can emerge here: communication distortion and communication lag.

Communication distortion refers to a situation where the intent of the message may be modified or changed by the time it gets to the worker. For example, a message about a need for immediate repairs may be reduced to a less important status by the time it has been communicated to the maintenance team. Communication lag refers to the fact that it may take considerable time for the appropriate workers to get the message, and meanwhile they are operating on one set of behaviours while management are expecting a different set. Clients may be the unintentional victims of these internal communication problems. Thus, a policy statement on rental arrears or allocations, by the time it reaches the shopfront workers, may have changed from a recommended status to a mandatory status as it is interpreted by different layers of the organisation.

Any organisation is essentially bureaucratic and evolves rules and regulations to suit the needs of the bureaucracy, which may be very different from the needs of the client. The worker caught between the requirements of the organisation and those of the client becomes the focus for conflict. As an employee of the organisation, the tendency is to side with the rules and procedures. The result may well be an alienated client group.

On the other hand, there is the dilemma that clients caught up in the challenges and difficulties of everyday life may not understand the wider role of housing agencies and the necessity for rules and procedures. Australian-born clients often express hostility to newly arrived migrants in public housing, seeing it as evidence of their ability to manipulate the rules. Stereotypes are often built around such misunderstandings (for example, the rich migrants who buy their way into public housing). The effect on tenants is to ‘prove’ the inequalities and inefficiencies of public housing management. Unfortunately, principles of priority housing and of obligations to refugees are either unknown or misunderstood by many clients.

Similarly, clients with a pressing housing need may believe that they should be housed immediately and find it difficult to accept waiting time or priority rules created by the organisation for reasons of fairness. The worker becomes the outlet for their anger.

4 Conflicting Worker/Client Expectations

Many workers have expectations, both of their work roles and of their clients, which clash with the clients’ expectations. A maintenance officer, for example, may see his or her role as one of fixing a physical problem such as damaged floor boards. The client may also have this expectation, but in addition may expect the worker to listen sympathetically to his or her problems – in other words, to be a de facto social worker. Failure to express interest disappoints the client, who subsequently complains about insensitive workers.

5 Difficult Clients

Some clients in public or community housing are simply difficult. One characteristic that many have is ‘time hedonism’, which may manifest itself in a hostile behaviour towards waiting. They see waiting as evidence of red tape, while an inability to understand the need for rules and regulations aggravates their impatience. They are unable to understand why workers cannot accept their word for the need for immediate action, and why supporting documentation is necessary (more red tape!). Such people may view planning ahead as unnecessary and tedious, and will expect assistance when they feel like visiting the office, even though a different time may have meant less queuing and frustration. By contrast, the worker may want very ordered and rule driven behaviour. Put the two together and there is a recipe for conflict.

Generally, it is the difficult clients who are constantly presenting themselves to workers, either directly – by making complaints – or indirectly – by being the households that others are complaining about. Non-difficult households (by far the greater number) rarely present themselves, so workers may develop a general view of clients which is based on those who do present as being difficult, and adopt management practices accordingly. Moreover, workers have to address the frustration that the community service obligations of social housing mean that difficult households – even those that may threaten assault or are selling drugs – cannot be pursued with the vigour or relentlessness that is more possible in the private rental sector. Even when action is taken through the Residential Tenancy Tribunals or courts, determinations often reflect the ‘circumstances of the case’ which may include the fact that problem tenants, if evicted, will have nowhere else to go. Such decisions are an understandable source of frustration for workers.

6 Personal Resentment

Most housing workers are on relatively low to reasonable incomes. Few receive the high incomes associated with senior management. Clients generally are on much lower incomes, and a sizable minority do not work for these incomes but are on various forms of income support. Some of these clients, despite lower incomes and a lack of employment, are in better housing than the workers themselves (some, such as single parents or single persons, at a relatively young age). It is therefore only natural that some workers may resent this situation and, not being fully aware of the circumstances that might have precipitated entry into public housing (for example, domestic violence) or of the broader objectives of social housing, will carry some of this resentment into their work setting.

7 Cultural Differences

Differences in culture can create problems in worker/client relations. These can take a number of dimensions, as the following examples illustrate:

  • A victim of torture and oppression may have a fear of authority figures. You as a worker are just such an authority figure. Will he or she deal with you with confidence?
  • A person from a society which shares a general belief that women should not be in positions of authority has to deal with a woman housing worker. Again, how successful do you think that worker/client relationship will be?
  • You have allocated a house to a person who has a strong belief in feng shui. The house infringes many of its principles and they want a reallocation. What do you do?
  • A household from a society that has no tradition of private property or of the concept of rent has fallen into arrears. How do you deal with this?
  • For some groups, the death of a household member means that the dwelling must be vacated for a period of time as part of the grieving process. The tenants have no expectation of paying rent in this period. What do you do?

The examples could continue, but these few highlight the difficult management task in dealing with people from a different culture, particularly when they may be presenting at a time of stress. It is also important to remember that all cultures are heterogeneous, and one cannot operate on the basis of stereotypes. Not every member of an ethnic group or of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities shares the same housing needs and values. Just as in our own culture (the reader can here define their own culture), there is considerable diversity. While some Aboriginals and Torres Strait Islanders may want a dwelling that is unconventional by white Australian standards and is designed to fit the needs of an extended family, others may want a conventional detached house on a quarter acre block. Being sensitive to another culture requires recognition of its diversity, not just that the culture may be different.

The problem referred to earlier of stereotypes about recent migrants is also a problem of cultural differences between social classes. Judged from the perspective of the wider society, many Australians born in social housing are ‘failures’ who have been – and may feel themselves to be – marginalised from that wider society. Some have evolved a culture that makes their position acceptable among their own peer groups or class (for example, a rejection of middle-class values) and adopt behaviours and views which may be anathema to workers who do not share that culture.

The concept of culture is not just of relevance in terms of ethnicity or social class. Every organisation attempts to develop a culture – that is, a distinct set of values, practices and beliefs which are broadly shared by many of its members – but it cannot be assumed that everyone in the organisation shares that culture. Many cooperatives and community housing groups have a philosophy or culture of tenant participation, believing in the empowering process that comes from participation and self-help, but not all members will share this. Some are in community housing only because it offers an affordable roof over their heads, and resent the time required to participate in decision making. This can be a source of tension and, unless there is tolerance of difference in values and needs, can create major management problems.

8 Stress at the Time of Negotiation

Clients normally require assistance only at a point of crisis, for example, failure of plumbing, falling into rental arrears, or a clash with a neighbour. When all is going well, no assistance is necessary. The problem here is that negotiations with housing workers are probably least well done when one is experiencing troubles or difficulties. The client is on the back foot from the start and will in all likelihood approach the negotiation with some trepidation, anger or other crisis induced behaviour. To the worker, such behaviour may come across as unrealistic and excessively demanding.

9 On First Appearances

The client’s first impression of a housing agency will probably come from the reception process, and how this is handled can set up a negative or positive experience in terms of worker/client relationships. Winning client support and creating impressions of quality service start at this point. The first time a client deals with the housing organisation will determine how they view that organisation. Telephones should be answered quickly and politely or, if there is a voice mail system, calls returned as soon as possible. Callers should know who they are talking to, and information should be provided clearly. Letters should be handled with equal promptness and clarity. In both situations, performance criteria can be implemented to facilitate improvement.

There should also be minimum conditions for reception areas and front offices, including:

  • Being well located;
  • Being welcoming;
  • Being sufficiently large for staff and clients;
  • Being secure for workers and comfortable for clients;
  • Staff should let tenants know how they will be dealt with, and in what order;
  • Leaflets about services provided by the housing authority or community agency should be available, along with leaflets about related services, for example, social security;
  • Publicity should be in clear English and in the ethnic languages used by clients;
  • Ideally, activities for young children should be provided, such as comics, books and soft toys;
  • Screens or counters separating staff and clients are not a good idea, but there should be an easy method for calling for help if necessary, such as alarms and back-up staff; and,
  • There should be separate space for confidential interviews, as most people would not want to talk about domestic violence or disputes with neighbours where they can be heard by others.

These issues do not get to the heart of the reception process. Many problems experienced at this level could be avoided if appropriate activities or strategies were adopted which reduced the need for direct staff/client interaction or provided clients with better information before confronting counter staff. For example, pamphlets in ethnic languages, while useful, should not be the sole publicity mechanism for programs. Ethnic newspapers and radio may be more effective.

10 Power Relationships

The staff/client relationship is a power relationship, consciously or unconsciously. The power derives from the housing worker’s control, however indirectly, over resources. As gatekeepers to housing, workers can affect how long a client waits, how much information they get, the manner in which that information is provided, whether they are referred on to anybody else, and how promptly their complaint or request is acted on. Clients can resent this uneven relationship and can interpret actions as exercises in power where no such intent was meant. The client’s only real sanction is behaviour; they can respond by being nice, aggressive, compliant, polite, rude or violent. More productive responses may occur where there is less visible expression of power on the part of workers.

11 Summary

This review draws out one obvious point: that the problems are not grounded in the characteristics of housing workers alone, but in the wider society, the organisational climate and the attributes of the clients. Better client services require reforms and change at all levels. To expect workers alone to create the conditions for a quality housing service would be misplaced and unfair.

C9 Arrears Management

All organisations which manage rental housing, whether in the private, community or government sectors, are faced with arrears (debts) owed to them by tenants and former tenants. Each organisation has to develop strategies to minimise debts and to manage and reduce them when they occur.

State Housing Authorities and community agencies may have detailed procedures manuals for housing workers or property managers whose job it is to manage arrears. Less commonly do they have a clear framework outlining the organisation’s policy on arrears, the role of arrears management in overall customer service, and the financial and political environment in which arrears management takes place.

2 Arrears and the Financial Viability of Housing Agencies

Community and government housing agencies do not exist to make a profit (capital gain and/or operating profit), as do private operators, although they may aim at a given return on assets. They generally aim to alleviate housing related poverty by providing affordable and secure housing for households on modest and lower incomes. Despite these more altruistic aims, all housing agencies are concerned with their financial viability, and uncontrolled arrears or a high level of arrears may threaten this. Moreover, a high level of arrears is an equity issue; the money foregone to the system means money not available to house other tenants.

3 Arrears from a Customer Perspective

3.1 Significance of Arrears for Customers

Arrears pose serious problems for tenants and ex-tenants as well as for housing agencies. They may exacerbate pressures on a household facing other financial or personal problems. Debt to a housing agency may limit or preclude options such as rehousing or transfer to more suitable accommodation. For debt relating to a current tenancy, the household’s tenure and stability may be thrown into doubt. This stability may be essential to maintaining affordability of housing, stability of a family unit, access to employment, and access to services and facilities.

However, despite the serious implications that arrears may have for customers and housing agencies, relatively little is known about why tenants fall into arrears. In lieu of adequate research on the causes, housing workers and others are often left to develop their own theories, a common one being the distinction between ‘can’t pay’ and ‘won’t pay’ customers. Workers may make their own judgements when households in apparently similar circumstances differ in their ability to pay.

3.2 Customer Circumstances

Government and community housing agencies have improved their targeting to the extent that the vast majority of tenants are on low incomes and are eligible for a reduced rent (rent rebate). Reduced rents are generally related to income and household composition and reflect the low income profile of tenants. Low income may mean that there is an insufficient buffer to meet even basic costs such as housing, and periodic crises may lead to arrears. Some of these circumstances could include:

  • Irregular or casual employment, leading to income fluctuations;
  • Unexpected costs – health; repair or replacement of old or unreliable items; fines; large bills which may be difficult to predict, for example, water charges;
  • Other commitments and priorities, such as payments for a car in areas of poor public transport; and,
  • Pressure to acquire consumer items, often on credit – advertisers, neighbours and friends may all add to the pressure to ‘buy now and pay later’.

It is important that housing agencies analyse arrears by household size, income type and area, to know more about the profile of those in arrears. There is some evidence, for example, that despite increased social security payments for children in recent years, larger families are more prone to fall into arrears.

3.3 Attitudes of Tenants

Some housing workers and others maintain that tenant attitudes to rent payment have changed over the years. It is commonly said that older people see rent as their highest priority, whilst many younger people see other payments as deserving priority. It is sometimes suggested that government and community housing agencies are seen as a ‘soft touch’ and that there is an expectation that nothing much will happen even if arrears continue to accumulate. There is no doubt that there are ‘problem tenants’ in terms of rent arrears, but whether they account for the bulk of them is unclear.

Some hypothesise that rental arrears are seen as a form of interest-free credit by tenants, although there is little definitive evidence. If true, it would imply skills in financial management by tenants, rather than the lack of skills in this area which is often assumed. Others have suggested that an emphasis on tenant and consumer rights, and a reluctance to proceed to the stage of eviction for political or philosophical reasons, may not encourage regular payment. Again, there is little hard evidence for this.

There is some suggestion that regular payment may be associated with tenant satisfaction with their homes. The rent rebate system means that rents are the same for similar households, whether they live in the most modern housing or the oldest or least appropriate. Tenants in poorer stock may feel less inclined to make regular payments. Where there have been ‘rent strikes’ in relation to current housing conditions, arrears accumulate rapidly and have been difficult to bring under control again.

3.4 Housing Agency Management Practices

Housing agency management practices may also contribute to the accumulation of arrears. Some argue that the cessation of local rent collection schemes (for example, small sub-offices, door-to-door collections or rent vans) has contributed to arrears increasing. There may be less personal contact between housing workers and tenants, with little opportunity to stem arrears before a larger and less manageable amount accumulates. Tenants may be less inclined to use alternative collection mechanisms on the fortnightly basis which is often required.

Perceptions of inaccuracy in rent or maintenance charges may contribute to arrears. Variations to rebates may appear to be retrospective but any additional income may already have been spent. The combination and frequency of review of gross rents and rebated rents may lead to confusion and affect payment. Maintenance charges may be considered to be unfair and may remain unpaid while they are being contested.

Staff turnover, workload and varied perceptions of the role and priority of arrears management may also contribute to an increase in arrears. Consistency of action is usually required to bring down arrears. Another factor may be the reliability and accessibility of information provided to housing workers. Immediate and up-to-date access to rental accounts aids arrears management, and this may not always occur. Targeted reporting of arrears is essential to ensure that changes can be dealt with quickly.

Fragmentation of aspects of housing management and poor coordination between housing agencies and various ‘partners’ in housing management may also contribute to arrears. In some cases, this is because of the time which elapses between initial arrears and agreement on the type of action to be taken. This may reflect differences in underlying philosophy between the housing agency and its management partner.

3.5 Issues Relating to Housing Agency Stock

Most housing agencies have a variety of stock, sometimes built up over many years. Some has been carefully designed to minimise ongoing costs to tenants. Much older stock has not been designed in this way, and tenants may be faced with higher than anticipated costs which may affect rent payment capacity, for example:

  • High energy costs due to construction material, orientation of housing etc.;
  • High user pays costs, such as high water use gardens coupled with pressure to maintain garden standards, particularly in drier regions; lack of drying facilities leading to drying and laundry costs, or expensive alternatives such as disposable nappies;
  • Costs of furniture – lift access or narrow stairways may mean that old and cheaper furniture will not fit;
  • Costs associated with providing furnishings – tenants may need to provide floor coverings, blinds or curtains;
  • Costs associated with security concerns, such as higher insurance premiums, difficulties in obtaining insurance, car insurance rates, costs associated with alarm systems or guard dogs for personal security;
  • Costs associated with vandalism, such as repair to cars, repair to uninsured items, premiums for call-outs; and,
  • Costs associated with the location of stock, such as transport costs.

The housing agency clearly has more direct control over its management practices and stock than over the other factors mentioned. It is important that arrears management is not merely seen as the efforts of housing workers in chasing up arrears. Effective arrears management will also depend upon other parts of housing agencies, including:

  • Provision of accurate information on arrears in a useable format;
  • Research into, and analysis of, arrears; and,
  • Informed discussion of the trade-offs between higher capital costs (solar heating, secure car parking, built-in wardrobes) versus lower ongoing costs for tenants with a low income profile.

In other words, a long-term asset management strategy may be an effective arrears management mechanism.

4 Arrears and the Social Objectives of Housing Agencies

The previous sections outlined why arrears are important both to housing agencies and customers. This section looks at the issues which housing agencies face in developing an overall framework for arrears management.

4.1 Legal and Political Context

Housing agencies operating rental housing are landlords and must comply with their rights and responsibilities under the residential tenancies legislation which applies in their State or Territory. Social housing workers may find the landlord role a difficult one. They may have become involved in the management of rental housing in the belief that they were fulfilling social objectives, such as providing more appropriate or better managed low rental housing. They may find balancing these objectives with the financial viability of the agency a difficult task.

Many housing agencies wish to be seen as ‘model’ or good landlords by customers and the wider community. However, strictly following the letter of residential tenancies legislation would lead them down a linear path culminating sometimes in recovering possession of properties through eviction of tenants as the ultimate means of managing arrears.

In practice, the linear path approach to arrears may often be tempered by other considerations. Housing Ministers and senior managers of housing agencies are faced with conflicting pressures which may lead them to intervene as arrears recovery procedures become more serious from the point of view of both the customer and the agency. These interventions are often not well received by workers whose job it is to recover arrears and who may see them as capricious or ‘soft’. In particular, interventions to ‘pull out’ on evictions at the eleventh hour are seen as lack of support for the efforts of housing workers and may be remembered for years afterwards.

Housing agency managers and ministers are aware of the need to achieve rent income and the problems of the rental operations deficit. They are also aware of the scrutiny by community advocates, the media and the general public when eviction is threatened or carried out in the name of arrears management. Ministers in particular tend to be sensitive to the confrontation displayed in eviction proceedings, especially where children are involved. Some may be reluctant to take away secure housing from households that perhaps owe only a few hundred dollars. For these reasons, last minute offers of payment (or part payment) may be accepted, even if offered by a third party. These decisions reflect concern that the primary purpose of the agency is to give people security in their housing, not to take it away.

On the other hand, the political climate today is one of ‘sound financial management’, and housing agency managers and ministers are keen to ensure that high or increasing arrears do not mean that they are seen as imprudent financial managers. From the housing workers’ point of view, decisions made at various review points may seem to be inconsistent, sometimes reflecting concern to be a model landlord and to achieve certain social objectives, and at other times driven by a fear of being found to be poor financial managers.

4.2 Eviction and Arrears Management

Housing workers and tenants sometimes feel that mixed messages are given out by housing agencies about the importance of arrears management and the particular techniques which the agency condones. This is hardly surprising in the light of the above discussion. On one hand, agencies seem to put forward their social objectives and emphasise customer service, yet, at times, issues of financial viability seem to be overriding. Perhaps the most extreme example of this can be found in attitudes towards eviction.

Some see eviction as necessary in hardcore arrears situations, to stop the accumulation of debt and to ‘remove the problem’. It is commonly believed that eviction is a deterrent to other tenants in arrears and encourages them to pay. Others find great difficulty with eviction as it runs counter to the social objectives for which the agency was established. They point out that some high-flying businessmen of the 1980s, for example, have had tens and sometimes hundreds of millions of dollars of debt written off and maintain their houses, whilst ‘little people’ can lose their homes over a debt of a few hundred dollars. Some see this as double standards.

Little is known about the effectiveness of eviction as a tool in arrears management, although there are plenty of anecdotes. Eviction stops debt accumulating further, but is unlikely to result in payment of arrears already owing. An evicted household is unlikely to see payment of arrears as a priority, and as a result of eviction they are even less likely to have the means to pay. Some housing workers welcome eviction as a means of ‘clearing the books’ of some current arrears. In fact, the debt does not disappear, it is merely transferred to ‘vacated arrears’, which are often the responsibility of some other part of the organisation. Vacated arrears are rarely given the attention of current arrears, and ex-customers’ whereabouts may be unknown. The recovery rate for vacated arrears is usually low, and an internal transfer of the debt in the agency’s books may be the main impact of eviction from the point of view of the cash deficit on rental operations.

More commonly, households simply vacate their homes under threat of eviction. Again, forwarding addresses are often not known, and maintenance charges may be incurred through lack of attention to the property at the time of vacation. The prospect of recovering the debt is remote. However, this action does stop the debt accumulating further, if this is the agency’s only objective.

Housing agency arrears procedures often include flow charts showing reminder letters as the beginning of arrears management and eviction as the end point. There is a danger of eviction coming to be seen as an end in itself, rather than enabling households to remain in their accommodation while managing and recovering their arrears.

In practice, households who vacate under threat of eviction or are evicted may seek further housing from the agency in the future. Most agencies specify that full repayment of the debt is required before housing is offered, but exceptions are made in situations of priority need, including domestic violence. At this stage, the housing agency again has to balance social objectives against reducing its rental deficit. The problem does not disappear with eviction or vacation under threat of eviction.

4.3 Setting an Overall Framework

In recent years, there has been an increasing awareness of customer or client service. Agencies have been giving greater attention to provision of better information, reliability in service provision, and respect for the rights of customers, including the right to confidentiality and privacy. Within this context, housing agencies must provide a clear framework for actions when customers are in arrears. In developing a framework, some of the questions that need addressing are:

  • What level of arrears does the agency consider acceptable?;
  • Which techniques for encouraging repayment of arrears are considered acceptable and which are not?;
  • What aspects of arrears management can be contracted out?; and,
  • Which model of arrears management does the agency want its workers to follow?

5 Arrears Prevention and Recovery

5.1 Arrears Prevention

Housing agencies have traditionally concentrated on arrears control and recovery, and comprehensive strategies to prevent arrears have been less common. Those considering arrears prevention (or encouragement to pay) might adopt a range of strategies across the agency, not only aspects of housing management. Some possible elements are as follows.

Incentives

Many commercial organisations offer incentives for customers who pay by the due date, such as cash discounts, competitions and prizes. Housing agencies have tended to concentrate on penalties for non-payment or late payment, rather than on incentives for regular or prompt payment. Incentives have been considered to be somewhat frivolous if they have been thought of at all. Some government bodies do use incentives, for example, local councils sometimes offer the chance to win a prize such as a holiday for ratepayers who pay by the due date.

It would be possible to identify incentives for regular and prompt payment by customers of housing agencies. These could include a week’s free rent at Christmas, some flexibility and choice in maintenance items, or entry into a lottery to win a house and land. Some argue that incentives only serve to reward the ‘slightly less poor’ at the expense of the ‘very poor’, whilst others see them as a possible reward for those who see rent payment as a priority. There is little information about the success of incentives in the public sector, and the private sector is often reluctant to make public the impact of particular incentive schemes.

Starting tenancies on a positive note

  • Notice to new tenants to prevent double payment of rent through overlapping tenancies;
  • Good induction of tenants, including explanation of rights and responsibilities, rent and service charges, third party charges (water) and maintenance;
  • Information on sources of assistance with relocation costs;
  • Information on rent payment options with regard to frequency and customer profile (elderly, people with disabilities etc.); and,
  • Early notification of documents which the customer requires to establish a reduced rent from the start of tenancy.

Giving priority to accuracy and reliability of information

  • Availability of rentals on new properties at the start of first tenancy;
  • Quick and accurate rents on existing properties;
  • Quick and accurate assessment of rebates (reduced rents) at the start of tenancy;
  • Quick and accurate reassessments of rebates during the course of tenancy;
  • Property condition reports, including at the start of tenancy as the basis for future maintenance charges;
  • Limit to the frequency of rebate assessments to avoid confusion, for example, averaging out of irregular income such as casual jobs or income of fluctuating value such as United Kingdom pensions; and,
  • Coordination of rent and rebate reviews to avoid confusion.

Speedy dispute resolution

  • Accurate documentation to justify charges, for example, property condition and damage reports;
  • Up-to-date and useable rental accounts information to help resolve disputes; and,
  • Commitment to speed in dispute resolution, for example, for third party debt or situations of ‘joint and several’ liability for debt where circumstances have changed.

Improved liaison with ‘management partners’

  • With third parties, for example, water authorities, where the landlord is responsible for debt; and,
  • With other agencies involved in housing management, including Aboriginal Housing Boards, community organisations.

Better design and planning

  • Consideration of the ongoing costs to tenants in all designs;
  • Consideration of the cost advantage/disadvantage of bulk energy systems;
  • Low water/low maintenance gardens, particularly in dry areas;
  • Orientation of housing to minimise energy costs;
  • Appropriate fixtures and fittings;
  • Durable fixtures, fittings and finishes in housing designed for children; and,
  • Identification and development of strategies for stock with poor tenant satisfaction.

These are only examples of how a housing agency might put together an overall strategy on arrears prevention. Each agency has its own objectives and style of operating and will develop its own preferred priorities. However, arrears prevention will be best seen as a responsibility across the agency.

5.2 Arrears Control and Recovery

There is little written on best practice in arrears control and recovery across housing agencies, although much time and effort is expended on these important tasks. Individual agencies have often had a number of detailed investigations which have established some principles:

  • Early intervention and contact, particularly for new tenants, before arrears get out of hand;
  • Checking the amount owed (incorrect amounts used in arrears action affect credibility), explaining this to the customer, and early and quick dispute resolution mechanisms;
  • Establishing agreements to repay when the debt is low enough, so that repayment is both affordable to the tenant and can be completed within a reasonable time;
  • Concentrating on early arrears and irregular rent payers, rather than on lengthy and time-consuming legal proceedings in hardcore cases;
  • Consistency and reliability in monitoring and following up; and,
  • Staff training in negotiation and interpersonal skills.

There is often work to be done in housing agencies to establish best practice principles in arrears control and recovery, and to ensure that they effectively guide detailed procedures manuals and actual contact with customers. Better management information systems should enhance this.

Strategies to control and recover arrears also require links with other activities of the housing agency, including:

  • How are applications from ex-tenants with prior debts to the agency treated?;
  • Are allocations made to ex-tenants with prior debts outstanding and under what circumstances?;
  • How are transfer requests from households with debts from current or prior tenancies treated? What happens if the agency wishes to transfer a household but debt exists?; and,
  • Are ex-tenants or current tenants with debts able to access other programs provided by the agency (such as private sector bonds, private rent assistance, home purchase schemes or land purchase incentives)?

The agency must draw up guidelines to deal with these situations, and information systems which enable crosschecks across program. It needs to establish a culture of collective responsibility for arrears control and recovery, in a similar way to the cross-agency responsibility for arrears prevention outlined above. Such a culture encourages adequate direction, support and back-up for frontline staff.

From the preceding sections it can be seen that, in order to manage arrears effectively, housing workers require a range of skills such as:

  • Clear understanding of the agency’s philosophy;
  • Focus on best practice, and willingness to share skills and experiences;
  • Understanding of, and involvement in, target setting;
  • Understanding of the agency’s customer base and of budgeting issues which affect low income earners;
  • Conflict management;
  • Negotiation skills;
  • Sound documentation skills;
  • Knowledge of referral agencies;
  • Understanding of the confidentiality and privacy aspects of debt management; and,
  • Ability to understand and use computerised information systems.

C2 Rebates and Subsidies

A social housing system is largely defined by its ‘not for profit’ status. The reason it does not make a profit is that it provides housing which is affordable for those on low incomes. How social housing ensures affordability differs from country to country. In most countries a form of cost rent (that is, a rent that covers the cost of providing the dwelling) is charged, not a market rent. If this is still not affordable, tenants typically receive a rent supplement or allowance from a central government authority to keep their rent below some benchmark, for example, 20 to 30 per cent of income.

Australia has a different system. Here a market rent is charged (see C3) and the housing agencies themselves give a rebate back to the tenant out of their revenue, ensuring in the process that actual rent paid is kept to some acceptable level such as 25 per cent of income. In practice, rebate cut-offs can be struck in three different ways: a flat rate of, say, 20 per cent across all income ranges; a sliding scale from some minimum, for example, 20 per cent at $300 per week to 25 per cent above $650 per week; and a stepped scale, for example, 20 per cent to $350, 25 per cent from $350 to $500, and 30 per cent above $500. In Australia, most States have had a sliding scale, although Queensland (up to 1994) and Tasmania had a stepped system for their income related rent subsidy mechanism.

1 Administering Rebates and Subsidies

A rebate relates not only to rents, but also to the tenant’s income. The degree of rebate is thus related to measures of income, with all the problems of defining and measuring eligible income. In the first CSHA formula set down in 1945, the rebate formula applied was based on the Commonwealth basic wage and the child endowment scheme. It was assumed that the basic wage was a relatively accurate measure of the cost of maintaining a minimum standard of living, and that the child endowment scheme removed the necessity for rents to take account of the number of children in the family.

The formula operated by measuring the ‘family income’ and then assessing capacity to pay. Where it exceeded the basic wage, rent was set at 20 per cent of income, but where it was less than the basic wage, the rent was one-fifth of the basic wage, plus one-third of the difference between the basic wage and the family income. For those on very low incomes (for example, half the basic wage), actual rents could be as low as 12 to 13 per cent of income.

With variations from State to State, this system remained in place until the late 1960s. Its relative generosity was enabled by the fact that only relatively few households were on rebates. As Table 1 shows, in the late 1960s these were around 15 per cent of all households, compared to around 90 per cent today. Some of the growth is explained by changes in eligibility (single parents were largely excluded from public housing prior to the mid-1960s), but mainly by demographic change, for example, marital breakdown and aging, and structural changes to the economy which have created higher house prices and rents and left greater numbers of unemployed and underemployed.

C3 Market Rents

Most social housing agencies in other countries charge a form of cost rent, that is, a rent related to the cost of provision of housing including amortised capital costs, depreciation and recurrent cost, including staff costs. By contrast, Australia charges a market rent, that is, what equivalent private sector housing is renting at. This divorces the rent from any relationship to actual production costs, and bases it on equivalent private market values. Given that the historic origin of social housing was to provide a tenure sector with a rent structure that was more affordable than market rents, it might seem something of a paradox to then set social housing rents at market values. The rationale is twofold.

Firstly, it is argued that any households not paying a market rent are receiving an implicit subsidy over and above any rent rebate paid. This is seen as a subsidy because, if a public sector house were rented out on the private market, it would bring the market rent of equivalent housing for that type and location. For example, if the market rent for a property was $150 but the household only paid $90 as a result of a rebate, then the total subsidy represents $60. Aggregated for all households, it is argued that a market rent method would then provide a benchmark by which to measure the subsidy for the social housing system as a whole. In short, it makes subsidies transparent. However, for most public sector households, so long as the rebate mechanism remains the same as at present, it is largely irrelevant whether there are cost or market rents. Only the minority of tenants not on rebates will receive the pricing signals provided by market rents.

Secondly, there are of course a minority (3 to 15 per cent, depending on State or Territory) of households in the social housing sector who are on good incomes. This occurs as a result of the process of time. People are allocated public housing at a low point in their employment careers, and hence it follows that their income earning capacity is at a low ebb. Some households do not remain at this ebb, and at a later date in their public housing occupancy may find that their employment and income earning capacity has greatly improved. It is argued that, as an equity issue, these households or individuals should not be subsidised by virtue of receiving a low rent, that is, those who can afford to do so should pay equivalent rents to those of people on the same incomes in the private sector. Another argument is that such households should not even be entitled to be in public housing, given the sizable waiting lists, and should be encouraged through market signals – that is, market rents – to exit.

A similar argument is used with respect to changing household circumstances. Over time, a household may move from child rearing, through to children leaving home, to loss of a partner and, finally, to single person status. The household in its various permutations may have been constantly resident in a three-bedroom dwelling, meaning that in its final permutation, for example, a couple or single person, it is underutilising the dwelling. A cost rent, being insensitive to housing characteristics, would not send any signals to tenants about their underoccupying a dwelling. By contrast, a market rent which reflected the size or locational aspects of the dwelling would send price signals which would encourage relocation to a more appropriate dwelling, thereby freeing the larger house for a larger family. This of course only applies for non-rebated households, as a rebate blunts the effect of any market signals, because the tenant feels no financial penalty after its application.

For either income or housing quality factors, it is argued that market rents would facilitate a more efficient use of a scarce resource as well as meeting both vertical equity goals (those on higher incomes pay more) and horizontal equity goals (those households on the same incomes in the private and public sector pay the same rent). This is the system that many SHAs and the community sector have switched to, or are switching to, and so it is important to expose it to more critical analysis than the other methods.

While there are good arguments for market rents, there are also arguments against them, including the problems of actually setting rents for properties that have few private market equivalents (for example, high rise) and the fact that they are subject to the vagaries of private market forces, which makes for some problems of management. For example, if a performance indicator is set such as proportion of household subsidy to total market rents, is the outcome being measured one of changes in cost to government of providing housing assistance, or is it just changes in market forces? Sales Policy

The sale of public housing was a fashionable policy response in the United Kingdom and the United States in the 1980s, and aroused considerable debate and attention. Yet it was old hat in Australia. We had started the process almost two decades earlier with the changes to the CSHA. The States endorsed the CSHA sales policy, but with varying degrees of support. Tasmania immediately rejoined the CSHA, and used CSHA funding to expand its already existing sales program. New South Wales and Victoria took the opportunity to introduce and substantially expand the sale of their CSHA funded public rental stock. South Australia was the least keen on selling its stock, and relied instead on its existing home ownership sales program, under which it built and sold dwellings to middle income applicants. While Queensland and Western Australia sold a significant minority of their CSHA stock, like South Australia they achieved most of their sales through existing home ownership schemes that had been established before the CSHA had been put in place.

From 1945 t0 1969, almost 90,000 CSHA dwellings were sold Australia-wide, with all but 3,500 being sold after the 1956 agreement. New South Wales and Victoria accounted for almost two-thirds of total dwellings sold. In some years in Victoria (1959-60 and 1960-61) and New South Wales (1956-57 and 1957-58), the number of CSHA dwellings sold exceeded the number being built. By the end of the 1960s, Victoria had sold 43 per cent of its public housing stock, while Tasmania had disposed of 67 per cent.

Awareness of the problems of selling stock brought an end to sales in the 1970s, except for relatively small numbers of stock that may have been surplus to requirements. In the late 1990s sales increased again in response to funding constraints. Most properties sold are in gentrifying inner urban areas where the sales value can purchase two or more properties elsewhere or be used for property upgrade. End C6

C4 Why Is Allocations Policy Needed in Social Housing?

Public and community housing exists in all advanced industrial countries because the private sector cannot provide housing that is affordable or appropriate for all households. This is partly because of the cost of such housing, and partly because of the low level of incomes and the social and personal disabilities of certain individuals and households. Before public housing emerged in Australia, that is, before World War Two, private housing was non-affordable for many households, and some was of such sub-standard quality that it affected the health not only of residents but of the wider community. This was because the conditions of much inner city housing in capital cities were so bad that thousands of houses were effectively slums. Pressures for affordable and appropriate housing gave rise to the State Housing Authorities and in the immediate postwar years to the Commonwealth-State Housing Agreement which provides funds for public housing authorities in each of the States.

From the original inception of public housing, some form of allocations policy was necessary. In 1946, State Housing Authorities typically had only a few thousand dwellings, and were overwhelmed by need. Given the tens of thousands of families who could not afford housing or were living in slum conditions, who among them were to be given housing? And once chosen, what housing were they to be given?

This allocations dilemma remains with State Housing Authorities today, as well as the community sector, but is a more difficult policy and administrative task because social housing now, compared to the early decades of the postwar era, caters for a greater diversity of households. Up until the mid-1960s, public housing was mainly for working, but low income, Australian-born households who were headed by an ex-serviceman. Migrants (except British) were generally ineligible, and only rarely was access considered for couples below 60 years of age with no children, single parent households, singles, young people or any group not showing ability for independent living. Today allocations policies encompass all household types, age groups and countries of origin. The system is fairer but, in consequence, more complex.

1 The Demand for Social Housing

There are many reasons why people desire social housing, but the lack of affordable housing is the dominant one. The scale of the affordability problem in Australia was suggested by the National Housing Strategy which found that in 1988 some 450,000 lower income units (roughly equivalent to a household) in the private sector had housing costs in excess of 30 per cent of their incomes, a level which is considered excessive and would warrant eligibility for public housing. This figure could be seen as representing the broad measure of public housing need in Australia.

By contrast, in 1997, there were only 382,000 public housing units in Australia, with a new construction rate and turnover rate that means roughly only some 51,000 new allocations can be made annually. These figures illustrate the huge potential need relative to supply, and why various ‘rationing’ methods are required to select those households who are to gain access to public housing. And we need to remember that the measure of need is an historical one, that is, 1988. Subsequent to that date, the restructuring of the economy, increasing unemployment and the adoption of policies that have widened income inequalities have probably substantially increased the number of households in housing need.

In 1997, approximately 240,000 households were on official waiting lists for public housing, compared to 99,000 in 1981. While New South Wales had the largest absolute number (around 93,000), South Australia had the largest relative number (some 36,000). More than half of the applicants nationally were single persons or sole parents. These numbers represent an enormous increase in demand over the last decade, despite the fact that only a minority of households who are potentially eligible for public housing actually apply. The housing rationing task is thus one of fitting 240,000 applicants into 51,000 vacancies.
