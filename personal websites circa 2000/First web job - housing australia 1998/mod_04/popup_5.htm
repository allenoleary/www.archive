4. The Dominance of the Detached House

5. New Urban Forms - The End of Detached Housing?

Many people still believe that what held for the past will hold for the future, and therefore our cities should continue as they have done for decades. This denies that the purchase costs of housing today, despite relatively low interest rates, are of a very different order to the period from 1950 through to the early 1980s; that high unemployment will be with us for the foreseeable future; that governments no longer have the discretionary funds for urban expansion; that the demographic structure and, therefore, housing needs are very different to those of past decades; and that Australian industry is going through a marked restructuring which has all sorts of income and employment implications.

The postmodernist Australia is simply one which will not enable the old urban form, based on the assumption of the detached house for all families, to be sustained to the degree of the past. This is not to suggest that urban consolidation is a universal and comprehensive panacea for Australia’s urban and economic problems. There are major problems with consolidation, and the reality is that the detached house on the fringe will continue to provide the bulk of new housing. Housing provision cannot be ahead of consumer preferences, and these are not changing so rapidly that the present increase in medium density housing can be heralded as a revolution which will see the end of the detached house.
