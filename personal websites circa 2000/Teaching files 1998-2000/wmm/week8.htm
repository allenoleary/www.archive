Writing for Multimedia

Class Eight: POV, world, user experience.

Allen O'Leary: alotronic@netspace.net.au.

Aims:

  • Examine the analysis and creation od character and dialogue.
  • Analyse the relationship between Point of View, World and User Experience.
  • Plan the POV and user relationship with the world.

Character

A character can be anything from a person to a pencil, as long as it has personality, embodiment and a function.

Characters perform various functions in any media:

  • Allow us to place ourselves in the events
  • Represent various ethical, emotional or conceptual views or states
  • Provide texture, setting, conflict

Characters have general parameters they share:

  • They want something from their lives (super-objective)
  • They want something in the piece we place them in (main line of action)
  • They want something from beat to beat (objectives and actions)

Characters are not generally motivated by passive things, even if their aims are negative, they want to achieve their aims. Desire is always a positive in that it leads to action and it breaks down over several levels. Desire provokes action, and contrary desires from various characters is conflict. Conflict itself in this light doesn't mean beating each other up, it means what happens when two or more active forces meet with differing goals. What happens then? Either someone changes or the conflict deepens to it's logical extreme. These moments where someone must change are often turning points in a drama - we want to see what the character does in response to opposition.Often a character will change how they act in a secxtion of a work, but will continue to pusue their major goals.

Exercise: Analyse an animation from both characters point of view and break down.

Exercise: Think about someone you know or that work colleague and see if you can break it down: What do they want from life, from their job. Think about a situation in which conflict was observed - was it resolved? Who backed down and why? Did anyones goals actually change?

Character Kit

Thinking about your work colleauge, try and work out the following:

  • Super-Objective
  • I see myself as (five keywords)
  • The world sees me as (five keywords)
  • If I were a car/food/alcoholic drink/sport/action hero/animal I would be a...
  • I dress to accentuate my...
  • I dress to hide my...
  • I lead with my (body part)
  • My idea of a good night is...
  • My idea of a bad night is...
  • I value my best friend because they
  • Favorite book/movie/tv-show/record/internet site (?)
  • Relaxing means...

Laban

Laban is a physical/kinaesthetic/emotive scale for tracking and exploring character:

  • Direct/Indirect (application of intention)
  • Broken/Sustained (attention or focus)
  • Light/Heavy (emotional weight)

These make up 6 (very) basic types

  • Slasher - indirect, broken, heavy
  • Dabber - direct, broken, light
  • Glider - direct, sustained, light
  • Presser - direct, sustained, heavy
  • Floater - indirect, broken, light
  • Wringer - indirect, sustained, heavy

These can be combined for more complex creations - for instance a Social Glider covering an Emotional Slasher (American Psycho!!). It's these combinations which make a character deep and compelling.

On top of this is added a physical center - what the character leads with.

Then there's the 'givens'.

  • Social Position
  • Age
  • Sex
  • Health
  • Physique

Interesting characters often 'go against', that is there will be interesting kinks or anomolies between the physical/social/emotional charateristics.

Dialogue

Dialogue is not natural, even when it seems that way. You have to choose the words to support the whole person but also the action. The action is much more important!

Exercise: Write out a short scenario for two characters It should have the basic dramatic shape:

  • A situation that establishes two characters
  • A revelation of conflicting goals
  • An escalation of conflict
  • A turning point
  • A resolution

Then add the dialogue and directions, page at the most.

Work on it with a partner.

  • Clarify the situation
  • Read it aloud playing the parts
  • Refine the dialogue

Point of View

In RL (real life) we have a constant Point of View and a constant, stable platform from which to experience life - our body and stereoscopic vision. In literary terms, we are all First Person all the time, occupying just the one body at a time. We supplement our sense of being with our imaginations, and use other techniques to allow us to identify with other people through the arts and film and so on, but still we have only one body.

Often through life we try and reinvent ourselves and make drastic changes to our superficial appearance or our circumstances through other jobs or further education, but we are aware that we take the whole lot (body, memories, prejudices) with us through life.

POV has always been heavily tied to the interface or performative medium of a work: it is physically not possible to have a first-person experience of a stage play for example, you are always forced into the third person experience. In theatre this has led to complicated narrative devices to develop empathy between the protagonist and the audience member.

Likewise in Multimedia, it's relatively easy to provide a first-person experience, but the visual nature of the screen makes plain-text a dull experience, something it is not in a printed novel, where the fluidity of the POV and narrators position - developed over a few centuries now - is a high point of our culture. The oldest forms of enlarging our own world view are the epic poem and the drama, and through drawing and personal ornamentation.

Over the centuries literature and religion became the two best ways to allow identification with others and, paradoxically, a way to leave our bodies and occupy other spaces, at least temporarily. The invention of TV and film have enlarged the vocabulary of drama and literature, but it's not until we got Multimedia that we see decisive shift in the way that people experience other states of being in that the convergence of media into one 'appliance' also merges the various ways that separate media have mediated our presence in abstract or artificial realities.

When analysing and making Multimedia we need to ask ourselves several questions about the relationship between the embodied reader/user, the virtual narrator/agent, and the world of the piece. Assembling this information can be difficult because Multimedia allows a larger variety of relationships than some media (such as film), and can add other media together, leading to subversive and difficult user/agent/world relationships. In fact it is often the invention of a new user/agent/world scheme that makes a work important, rather than the works content. This is because we are still trying to work out how interactive media works, and still expanding the numbers and types of interfaces.

POV is usually about the relationship between user and agent/world, rather than agent and world, because the agent partakes of their virtual world in the same way that we partake of ours: first person singular. It is the nature of the bridge between the user and the avatar/world that is important.

Methods

Finding out more about POV (do this in class with Allen)

1. Make a list of all the user position descriptions you can think of. What are the qualities of each of the positions - how do they enhance the experience for the user, or help them achieve their goals? Think about the qualities of empathy, immersion, excitement, kinesthetic involvement, control, abstract thought, organisation, problem solving.

2. List some Multimedia titles including games that you can think of or have played/owned. Group them into 1st, 2nd and 3rd person user positions - does the user position change in the course of a piece? If so, why?

3. Note down points about each of the user positions, how does each title use user position as a way to enhance it's appeal or message? How would each title be changed if the user position were shifted to another?

Developing a User/Agent/World schema - how do they interact and why? (do for your project)

The trick here is to identify how the world, user and agent will be unified into a seamless experience through the integration of the users task and the world by determining both who the main agent is and then the relationship between the user and the agent, and then the agent with the world.

Later on we develop these ideas into an interface design. Consider the following points:

Refer back to the aim of your piece and make a note of it. Look over your notes for the type of world you are developing.

You need to decide who the main agent/agents are - who is it that is going to be the users representative in this piece and why? Why this particular agent amongst the many in your world? Does the user have one agent or can they switch, and if so, why?

Think of the type of experience you are trying to get your user to have and see if any of the POV's we have looked at and the types of experience they encourage make a good fit. For instance, in an action piece you may choose a 1st person user/agent relationship to heighten the excitement and experience of movement and action. If your agent is considerably different from your target user in age or type, you may want to use a 2nd person (avatar) relationship to encourage the user to remember who the agent is (you might take this approach with a 'Little Red Riding Hood' piece).

Consider also the role of time in your selection - how does time move in the game? Is it turn based? Real time? Does time pass at all? · Do you need to offer the user opportunities to shift from one user/agent/world position to another in the course of the piece? In some games it is common to have 'command' screens where users can select forces and then a playing screen which has a real time clock and an active opponent. This sets different relationships between the modes: the first is better for organisational thinking, while the second pressures the user into action.

Think about all the above points and write out a User/Agent/World schema by:

1. Identifying the primary user goals

2. Identifying the primary agent/agents the user controls - why this/these particular agent?

3. Noting the primary relationship between the user and the agent in terms of POV - why choose this relationship?

4. Noting when the POV relationship shifts and why.

From here we will be ready to develop the main agents character and also extend our POV decisions into an interface design.
