La Chambre d'Amour

La Chambre d'Amour takes its name from a large black rock where two lovers, surprised by the rising tide, were discovered drowned.
A multi-media play exploring this medieval legend from the Basque French country-side presented through different perspectives of time.
The play was commissioned for the STAB Multi-media Festival on the theme of Art and Technonogy. Combining film images (16mm and video) with live performance, Bats Theatre was completely transformed to the needs of the production (projections on the walls and a convex parachute above the audience), with the principal action happening mid air (suspended balcony, twin stairs, swings) and under water (giant aquarium).
WRITTEN, DIRECTED & DESIGNED BY  France Hervé and Stephen Bain
CAST  France Hervé & Stephen Bain
VIDEO MONTAGE & ORIGINAL MUSIC France Hervé & Stephen Bain

ADDITIONAL MUSIC

Franck Dematteis
PREMIERE  Bats Theatre Wellington 2 October 1996
Commissioned by Bats Theatre STAB festival

"Images are screened to the side, above and beyond the audience and film is screened through a water tank, in which Bain and Hervé swim underwater. There is a beautiful lyric sensibility and some astonishing theatrical devices… some of the most beautiful image-making you'll see this year."

Capital Times, Oct '96

"For La Chambre d'Amour the impression of brightness is enhanced by the use of water, light reflected from goldfish bowls placed on white wall sconces, and an enormous glass tank of water. Seated on the floor of what is usually the stage, the audience has its attention drawn to every part of the theatre in Hervé and Bains' original and highly inventive use of the space. Film of rock is shown on the concave ceiling and phrases in French and English flash in the opposite wall. It is quite beautiful, obeying its own logic in its interconnections.
La Chambre d'Amour is described as "an exploration of the myth through the dynamic of time." It travels aeons from pre-history with life emerging from the teeming soup of the oceans to the present in which two lovers hold each other in complete trust and desire for an eterneity such of love. Then it moves to a Kafka-like future in which a computer can hold the details of the beloved's life apart from the deepest truths, to a gloriously mythical time in which the sun and the moon, in perfect counterpoise, swing high above us hurling insults at each other."

The Dominion, 4 Oct '96
