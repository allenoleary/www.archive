Writing for Multimedia

Class Seven: Worlds

Allen O'Leary: alotronic@netspace.net.au.

Aims:

  • Analyse what makes a world
  • Analyse and describe your own world
  • Describe an imaginary world

Methods:

Analyse what makes a world

We take our being in the world completely for granted, but what are the physical, social and personal factors that make up our perception of the world? What rules do we have to abide by in each realm? What are the 'givens' in our own interaction with the world? What factors are we able to change and in what circumstances? What makes for enjoyment of our world? What factors are ambient and what are particular and why?

Exercise One: You and your world (1 hr)

Go outside, walk around for 35 minutes thinking about the following things, then spend about 30 minutes addressing the following points in writing:

Physical World

What the rules I must abide by? What are consequences or opportunities of breaking the rules of the physical world? How do you find out what the rules of the physical world are?

Social World

What the rules I must abide by? What are consequences or opportunities of breaking the rules of the social world? Where are the social rules written? How do you learn them? Are the social rules here different from where you grew up? How many different social realms do you partake in? How do you go from one realm to another?

Personal World

What are some of the rules that you set yourself (ethics)? When do you break your own rules and why? What activities do you enjoy and why? Do these activities have their own rules? What sytles, aesthetics do you like and why?

Stop for a moment on your walk and briefly describe:

What you are seeing
What you are hearing
What you smell
What you hear
What you taste
Any movement in the environment
What you feel towards the environment you are in

Techincal World

What tools do you carry with you every day? What tasks do they accomplish? Would you like better tools? What does that mean? (A cell phone that you could write into?). Also look at the aesthetics of your tools - when does their functionality blur with your personal aesthetic?

Exercise 2 (1 hour)

Taking some of the things you have explored above, start writing up a description of the world of your Littel Red Riding Hood piece.

Start by placing yourself in the ambient world of your piece and imagine the sensations that you are feeling as the main agent: what you see, smell etc. Then imagine and record the following information about your world, using the table to help you address all the options:

Worlds: Physical, Social, Personal

Physical

Being

What are the dominant species of the world? Can they change shape or form? How long do they live? What size are they? Draw a picture.

Movement

How do you move in your world? Are there different intensities of movement, such as walking, running, flying… How about gravity?

Survival

Are there any special rules of survival? Do you need extra equipment to survive?

Time

How does time progress? Is it absolute or subjective? Does time play a part in the makeup of your world?

Transport

Are there any forms of transport? How do they work? Who has them?

Landforms

Describe the terrain of the world, is it hospitable? What is there a lot of? Not much of? Draw a picture.

Colour and Sense

What is the dominant palette of this world? What are the ambient sounds, textures, smells and tastes of this world?

Technical What tools are carried? What are the tasks that they accomplish?

Social

Class or caste

Is there a class or caste system? Are the boundaries firm or flexible?

Communication

What are the means of communication? Who owns them? Does eveyone have access?

Type of government

Is this a monarchy, an autocracy, a democracy, a dictatorship, a commune, a cult? What effect does this form of government have on the inhabitants of this world?

Built environment

How much if any of the world is urbanised? Is there a rural sector? A wilderness? Are the urban forms well inhabited? In good repair? Draw a picture.

Social Fabric

Are things generally good or generally bad? What makes them so? Who has power? Do they use it well?

Belief

Is there a predominant or well established belief system? Who believes and why? Is the belief system tied to that of government?

Play

What do people do for fun? Sport? Culture? Art?

Life pattern

What is the general pattern of life? Does everyone experience the same general pattern? Who are the exceptions and why?

Family

Is there a family system? Are people brought up by their parents or others? Is the family unit regarded as healthy?

Fashion

Is there a fashion system? What do different types of people wear? Draw.

Personal (for the main character or primary agent)

Your appearance

Are you typical in appearance? What do you wear? Draw.

Your social standing

Where in the social mix are you placed? Are you tied there? Do you have much money?

Your aim in life

What's the main thing you want out of life?

Your place in the world

Taking in all the above information, where are you in relation to the world, and it's social structures? Are you a 'winner'? Are you a typical resident?

Strengths & weaknesses

Relative to the rest of the world, what are your strengths and weaknesses? Do you have any special perceptions or actions that no one else has? Do you lack something everyone else has?

Communication

Do you communicate in the usual way? What sort of words or gestures do you use?

Sensory preference

What sense do you prefer to use? Why?

Perception, memory

How do you gather and store information? Do you have any forms of artificial memory? Does everybody have them?

When you are finished with this, you should have a good idea of the makeup of the world you have for your piece. You can exploit this information in may ways, but mostly it will help you develop a consistent, interdependent world with a degree of believability that will enhance the absorption your user feels towards your piece.

Notes for Al:

Flow: the psychology of optimal experience:

A challenge requiring skills
A chance of completion
The opportunity to concentrate, merging action and awareness
Clear goals
Deep involvement transcending distractions and the awareness of time
A sense of control over actions
Absorption
Expression of self through experience

 

Our own lives fall short, so we create

  • Consistency of the world
  • Limitations
  • Interdependent - that is aspects of the world should affect each other
  • Multi-sensorial
  • Quality of immersion: the more you imagine, the 'thicker' the world will be.
